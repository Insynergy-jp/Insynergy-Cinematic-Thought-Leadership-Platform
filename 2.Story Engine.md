# 2.1 Story Engine

The Story Engine is the intellectual core of the platform.

Its responsibility is **not** to summarize an article.

Its responsibility is to discover the dramatic story hidden inside an institutional idea.

Every downstream component — Screenplay, Shot Planning, Rendering — depends on the artifacts produced here.

This section defines the Story Engine as a formal architectural subsystem, decomposed into normative chapters.

---

# 2.1.1 Story Engine Goals & Objectives

## Insynergy Cinematic Thought Leadership Platform

### Master Specification v2.0

---

# Purpose

This section defines the qualitative and quantitative objectives that the Story Engine shall satisfy.

Story quality is treated as a **first-class architectural requirement**, equal in importance to visual quality, rendering performance, and system reliability.

The Story Engine shall optimize **dramatic strength**, **conceptual integrity**, **determinism**, and **downstream stability** without degrading the institutional accuracy of the source article.

A trailer can only be as strong as the story beneath it. No amount of cinematic rendering can recover a weak narrative.

---

# Design Philosophy

The Story Engine follows one fundamental rule.

> **Ideas become memorable only after they become human experiences.**

Accordingly, the Story Engine transforms:

* concepts
* frameworks
* governance theories
* institutional structures

into:

* people
* goals
* obstacles
* decisions
* consequences

Optimization of prose or vocabulary is considered secondary. The primary objective is the extraction of a single, dramatically viable human experience.

---

# Primary Story Objectives

The Story Engine shall satisfy the following objectives.

| Objective              | Target                         | Priority |
| ---------------------- | ------------------------------ | -------- |
| Dramatic Premise       | Exactly 1                      | Critical |
| Protagonist            | Exactly 1                      | Critical |
| Dramatic Question      | Exactly 1, unresolved to Act 3 | Critical |
| Supporting Roles       | ≤ 3                            | High     |
| Primary Theme          | Exactly 1                      | Critical |
| Three-Act Structure    | Fully generated                | Critical |
| Measurable Stakes      | ≥ 1 quantified loss            | Critical |
| Concept Ratio          | ≤ 0.20                         | High     |
| Story Generation Time  | ≤ 30 sec                       | High     |

The target environment assumes deterministic, cacheable execution within the platform Build Pipeline.

---

# Success Definition

A story is considered successful only if all of the following conditions are met:

* A single dramatic premise is produced.
* Exactly one protagonist carries the narrative.
* Stakes are measurable rather than vague.
* One dramatic question remains unresolved until Act 3.
* A continuous emotional arc is generated.
* All Story Quality Gate conditions pass.
* Story artifacts are immutable and deterministic.

Producing readable prose alone does not constitute success. A story that summarizes rather than dramatizes shall be rejected regardless of language quality.

---

# Story Quality Budget

Each stage of the Story Engine is allocated a maximum execution budget.

```yaml
story_quality_budget:

  argument_extraction:
    max_duration: 20s

  theme_detection:
    max_duration: 10s

  dramatic_question:
    max_duration: 10s

  premise_generation:
    max_duration: 15s

  logline_generation:
    max_duration: 10s

  character_design:
    max_duration: 20s

  conflict_design:
    max_duration: 15s

  stakes_design:
    max_duration: 10s

  three_act_structure:
    max_duration: 20s

  quality_gate:
    max_duration: 10s
```

Stages exceeding their allocated budget shall emit a performance warning without blocking the build.

---

# Draft Story Target

Draft stories exist to validate dramatic intent rather than final narration polish.

Objectives:

* Validate protagonist viability
* Validate conflict presence
* Validate stakes measurability
* Validate act ordering
* Minimize generation cost

Target characteristics:

```yaml
draft:

  target_duration:
    5-15 seconds

  quality_profile:
    draft

  validation:
    relaxed

  cache_usage:
    maximum
```

Draft generation favors structural validity over narration refinement.

---

# Canonical Story Target

Canonical stories prioritize production readiness.

Objectives:

* Produce publication-ready dramatic premise
* Preserve institutional accuracy
* Validate thematic consistency
* Produce immutable downstream inputs

Target characteristics:

```yaml
canonical:

  target_duration:
    15-30 seconds

  quality_profile:
    canonical

  validation:
    strict

  immutability:
    enforced
```

Once a canonical story is approved, its artifacts must never change during rendering.

---

# Story Quality Objectives (SQO)

The Story Engine defines measurable quality objectives.

```yaml
sqo:

  dramatic_score:
    >=0.85

  conflict_score:
    >=0.80

  stakes_score:
    >=0.85

  emotional_progression:
    >=0.85

  concept_ratio:
    <=0.20

  premise_uniqueness:
    ==1

  protagonist_count:
    ==1
```

Persistent deviation from these objectives requires narrative architecture review.

---

# Key Story Indicators (KSI)

Operational dashboards shall report, at minimum:

* Dramatic Score
* Conflict Score
* Stakes Score
* Emotional Progression Score
* Concept Ratio
* Story Generation Time
* Argument Extraction Time
* Theme Detection Time
* Story Cache Hit Ratio
* Story Cache Miss Ratio
* Quality Gate Pass Rate
* Rejection Rate by Forbidden-Story Category

All KSIs shall be recorded for historical trend analysis.

---

# Story Constraints

## Constraint 1 — Human First

Every story must center on a human decision.

Concepts, frameworks, and institutions may appear only in service of that decision.

## Constraint 2 — Determinism

Given identical inputs, the Story Engine shall produce identical artifacts.

Randomness is forbidden in the canonical path.

## Constraint 3 — Single Premise

Exactly one dramatic premise may exist per story.

Competing premises shall be resolved before downstream handoff.

## Constraint 4 — Immutability

Approved story artifacts are immutable.

Downstream modules consume them but never modify them.

## Constraint 5 — Observability

Every stage shall emit metrics, scores, and a decision log.

Unobservable narrative decisions are prohibited.

---

# Architectural Story Priorities

The Story Engine resolves conflicts between objectives using the following priority order.

```text
Dramatic Integrity

↓

Institutional Accuracy

↓

Determinism

↓

Generation Speed
```

Speed shall never be improved at the expense of dramatic integrity.

---

# Acceptance Criteria

Section 2.1.1 is complete when:

* Story quality is formally defined as a first-class objective.
* Primary story objectives are quantified with targets and priorities.
* Success is defined by dramatic viability, not prose quality.
* A per-stage quality budget is established.
* Draft and canonical story targets are distinguished.
* Story Quality Objectives (SQO) and Key Story Indicators (KSI) are enumerated.
* Story constraints and architectural priorities are documented.
* Every downstream module can be evaluated against a common story-quality contract.

# 2.1.2 Argument Extraction & Thematic Analysis

## Insynergy Cinematic Thought Leadership Platform

### Master Specification v2.0

---

# Purpose

This section defines the first analytical stage of the Story Engine: the extraction of dramatic potential from an institutional article.

This stage converts unstructured argument into structured narrative raw material.

It is responsible for three outputs: a structured argument map, a single dominant theme, and a single dramatic question.

Every later stage — premise, logline, character, conflict — depends on the correctness of this stage.

---

# Design Philosophy

This stage follows one operating rule.

> **Separate facts from dramatic potential.**

An article contains claims, evidence, and examples. Most of it is not dramatic.

The task is not to preserve the article. The task is to locate the single institutional tension that can become a human story.

Accordingly, this stage discards material that carries no dramatic potential, and preserves only what can generate conflict, stakes, or a decision.

---

# High-Level Flow

```text
Article

↓

Argument Extraction

↓

Theme Detection

↓

Dramatic Question

↓

(handoff to Premise Generation)
```

Each step is deterministic and independently testable.

---

# Inputs

## Primary Input

```yaml
Article
```

Contains:

* title
* subtitle
* article body
* metadata
* references

## Secondary Input

```yaml
Author Style
```

Contains:

* recurring themes
* terminology
* tone
* fixed definitions

Author Style constrains theme naming and terminology but must never override institutional accuracy.

---

# Outputs

This stage produces the following artifacts.

```text
argument-map.json

theme.json

dramatic-question.json
```

Each artifact is immutable once emitted and becomes an input to Premise Generation.

---

# Argument Extraction

Purpose

Separate facts from dramatic potential.

Input

Article

Output

```json
{
  "claims": [],
  "evidence": [],
  "examples": [],
  "institutional_problem": "",
  "proposed_solution": "",
  "dramatic_candidates": []
}
```

The `dramatic_candidates` field records fragments that carry latent conflict, ranked for downstream use.

---

# Claim Classification

Every extracted claim shall be classified.

| Class          | Definition                                  | Dramatic Value |
| -------------- | ------------------------------------------- | -------------- |
| Factual        | Verifiable institutional fact               | Low            |
| Structural     | Describes an institutional mechanism        | Medium         |
| Tension        | Exposes a conflict, gap, or failure         | High           |
| Consequence    | Describes a loss or irreversible outcome    | High           |

Only Tension and Consequence claims may seed a dramatic candidate.

Factual and Structural claims provide context but never drive the narrative.

---

# Evidence & Example Handling

Evidence and examples are retained only when they support a Tension or Consequence claim.

Rules:

* Evidence unlinked to a tension claim is discarded.
* Examples are converted into concrete, human-scale situations.
* Statistics are preserved only if they can become measurable stakes.

Example

Wrong

```text
Retain every statistic in the article.
```

Correct

```text
Retain only the statistic that quantifies the loss.
```

---

# Institutional Problem Detection

Every article shall yield exactly one institutional problem.

```json
{
  "institutional_problem": "Authority exists but no one designed it.",
  "proposed_solution": "Decision Design",
  "problem_class": "governance"
}
```

If multiple candidate problems exist, the stage selects the one with the highest dramatic potential and records the others as rejected candidates.

If no institutional problem can be detected, the stage fails fast (see Failure Handling).

---

# Theme Detection

The Story Engine extracts exactly one dominant theme.

Examples

| Article       | Theme                              |
| ------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| Authority     | Responsibility without authority   |
| Cybersecurity | Trust under uncertainty            |
| AI Governance | Invisible institutional structures |
| ERP           | Delegation and accountability      |

Only one primary theme may exist.

Secondary motifs may be recorded but shall never compete with the primary theme downstream.

---

# Theme Scoring

Candidate themes are scored and the highest-scoring candidate is selected deterministically.

```yaml
theme_scoring:

  dramatic_potential:
    weight: 0.4

  institutional_accuracy:
    weight: 0.3

  audience_relevance:
    weight: 0.2

  uniqueness:
    weight: 0.1
```

Output

```json
{
  "primary_theme": "Invisible institutional structures",
  "theme_score": 0.91,
  "rejected_themes": []
}
```

Ties are broken by institutional accuracy, then by declaration order, preserving determinism.

---

# Dramatic Question Generation

Every story must revolve around one dramatic question.

The question shall be phrased as a human question, not a conceptual one.

Wrong

> What is Decision Design?

Correct

> Who designed authority?

Rules:

* The question must be answerable only by a human decision.
* The question must remain unresolved until the climax.
* Exactly one dramatic question may exist.

Output

```json
{
  "dramatic_question": "Who designed the authority the system is using?",
  "resolved_in_act": 3
}
```

---

# Stage Contract

This stage conforms to the standard Story Engine stage interface.

```python
class StoryStage:

    prepare()

    execute()

    validate()

    finalize()
```

Stages must not invoke downstream stages directly.

The Story Orchestrator manages all transitions.

---

# Data Contracts

This stage produces, for each run:

* one primary artifact
* one metadata file
* one decision log

Example:

```text
argument-map.json

argument-map.meta.json

argument-map.log
```

The decision log records which claims were promoted to dramatic candidates and why, enabling reproducibility and review.

---

# Failure Handling

The stage shall fail fast under the following conditions.

```yaml
failures:

  no_institutional_problem:
    action: reject
    reason: "Article contains no dramatizable tension."

  multiple_primary_themes:
    action: resolve_by_score
    reason: "Only one primary theme is permitted."

  no_dramatic_question:
    action: reject
    reason: "No human decision can be derived."

  concept_only_article:
    action: reject
    reason: "Article is a concept list, not a story source."
```

A rejection halts the Story Engine before premise generation and returns an explicit narrative-failure report.

---

# Stage Quality Gate

Before handoff, the stage must satisfy the following gate.

```yaml
argument_stage_gate:

  institutional_problem_exists: true

  primary_theme_count: 1

  dramatic_question_count: 1

  dramatic_candidates_present: true

  concept_only: false
```

Failure of any mandatory item blocks downstream premise generation.

---

# Anti-Patterns

The following patterns are prohibited in this stage.

## AP-201 Article Summarization

Wrong

```text
Article

↓

Condensed article
```

Correct

```text
Article

↓

Single dramatic tension
```

Summarizing the article is not story extraction.

## AP-202 Multi-Theme Extraction

Wrong

```text
Extract every theme present.
```

Correct

```text
Select exactly one dominant theme.
```

Competing themes destabilize every downstream stage.

## AP-203 Concept-First Question

Wrong

> What is the framework?

Correct

> Who is responsible when the framework fails?

A dramatic question must resolve through a human decision, not a definition.

---

# Interfaces

This stage exposes the following components.

```python
ArgumentExtractor

ClaimClassifier

InstitutionalProblemDetector

ThemeDetector

ThemeScorer

DramaticQuestionGenerator
```

Each component is independently testable and produces deterministic output.

---

# Acceptance Criteria

Section 2.1.2 is complete when:

* Argument extraction separates factual material from dramatic potential.
* Claims are classified and only Tension and Consequence claims seed drama.
* Exactly one institutional problem is detected per article.
* Exactly one primary theme is selected deterministically by score.
* Exactly one dramatic question is generated and remains unresolved until Act 3.
* Stage and data contracts are defined and reproducible.
* Failure conditions fail fast with explicit reasons.
* Anti-patterns are documented and enforceable.
* Downstream premise generation depends only on this stage's immutable artifacts.

# 2.1.3 Dramatic Premise & Logline

## Insynergy Cinematic Thought Leadership Platform

### Master Specification v2.0

---

# Purpose

This section defines the stage that converts structured analysis into a compressed dramatic statement.

It produces two artifacts: the dramatic premise and the logline.

The premise explains **why the story exists**. The logline expresses **what the audience will experience** in a single sentence.

Both artifacts are immutable once approved and constrain every downstream creative decision.

---

# Design Philosophy

This stage follows one operating rule.

> **A story that cannot be stated in one sentence cannot be filmed in one trailer.**

Compression is not summarization. Compression forces the story to declare its protagonist, its tension, and its irreversible consequence.

If any of these cannot be expressed, the underlying story is incomplete and shall be returned to earlier stages.

---

# High-Level Flow

```text
argument-map.json

theme.json

dramatic-question.json

↓

Dramatic Premise

↓

Logline

↓

(handoff to Character Engine)
```

Each output is deterministic given identical inputs.

---

# Inputs

This stage consumes only Story Engine artifacts.

```text
argument-map.json

theme.json

dramatic-question.json
```

The original article is **not** an input. All required dramatic material must already exist in the analysis artifacts.

---

# Outputs

```text
dramatic-premise.json

logline.json
```

Both artifacts become immutable inputs for the Character Engine.

---

# Dramatic Premise

Definition

A one-paragraph description explaining why the story exists.

Structure

```yaml
premise:

  protagonist_situation:

  discovery:

  institutional_tension:

  irreversible_deadline:

  required_decision:
```

Example

```text
An enterprise risk director discovers that an autonomous AI system
is about to approve a legally significant contract.

The system knows what it is authorized to do.

Nobody can explain who designed that authority.

Before the transaction becomes irreversible,
she must decide whether the problem is the AI,
or the institution itself.
```

---

# Premise Constraints

The dramatic premise shall satisfy the following constraints.

```yaml
premise_constraints:

  paragraph_count: 1

  protagonist_present: true

  institutional_tension_present: true

  irreversible_deadline_present: true

  decision_required: true

  concept_first: false
```

A premise that opens with a concept rather than a human situation shall be rejected.

---

# Logline

Maximum length

50 words.

Structure

```text
When <incident>

a <protagonist>

must <goal>

before <stakes>

otherwise <loss>.
```

Example

```text
When an autonomous AI begins approving a critical contract,

an Enterprise Risk Director must stop it

before the transaction becomes legally binding,

or discover that no one truly owns the authority to intervene.
```

---

# Logline Constraints

```yaml
logline_constraints:

  max_words: 50

  incident_present: true

  protagonist_present: true

  goal_present: true

  stakes_present: true

  loss_present: true

  question_form: false
```

The logline shall be declarative. A logline phrased as a question is rejected.

---

# Premise-to-Logline Consistency

The logline must be derivable from the premise.

Rules:

* The protagonist named in the premise must appear in the logline.
* The irreversible deadline in the premise must map to the logline stakes.
* The required decision in the premise must map to the logline goal.

Inconsistency between premise and logline blocks the stage.

---

# Stage Contract

```python
class StoryStage:

    prepare()

    execute()

    validate()

    finalize()
```

The stage validates premise-logline consistency during `validate()` before emitting artifacts.

---

# Data Contracts

This stage produces, for each run:

* one premise artifact
* one logline artifact
* one decision log

```text
dramatic-premise.json

logline.json

premise.log
```

The decision log records which dramatic candidate seeded the premise.

---

# Failure Handling

```yaml
failures:

  no_protagonist_situation:
    action: return_to_argument_stage
    reason: "Premise has no human anchor."

  no_irreversible_deadline:
    action: return_to_argument_stage
    reason: "No time pressure can be expressed."

  logline_exceeds_limit:
    action: regenerate
    reason: "Logline exceeds 50 words."

  concept_first_premise:
    action: reject
    reason: "Premise opens with a concept, not a situation."
```

---

# Stage Quality Gate

```yaml
premise_stage_gate:

  premise_paragraph_count: 1

  logline_word_count_max: 50

  premise_logline_consistent: true

  decision_required: true
```

Failure of any mandatory item blocks the Character Engine.

---

# Anti-Patterns

## AP-301 Summary Premise

Wrong

```text
This story is about AI governance.
```

Correct

```text
A risk director must stop an AI before it binds the institution.
```

A premise describes a human under pressure, not a topic.

## AP-302 Question Logline

Wrong

> What happens when authority has no owner?

Correct

> A director must find who owns authority before the contract binds.

The logline states the stakes; it does not ask them.

---

```python

```

Each component is independently testable and deterministic.

# Acceptance Criteria

Section 2.1.3 is complete when:

* Exactly one dramatic premise is produced per story.
* The premise contains a protagonist situation, institutional tension, irreversible deadline, and required decision.
* Exactly one logline of at most 50 words is produced.
* The logline is declarative and derivable from the premise.
* Premise-logline consistency is validated before handoff.
* Failure conditions return control to earlier stages or regenerate as specified.
* The Character Engine depends only on the immutable premise and logline artifacts.

# 2.1.4 Character Engine

## Insynergy Cinematic Thought Leadership Platform

### Master Specification v2.0

---

# Purpose

This section defines the stage that converts a dramatic premise into human characters.

The Character Engine produces the character bible: exactly one protagonist and a bounded set of supporting roles.

Characters are the vehicle through which institutional ideas become experiences. Without a viable protagonist, no downstream stage can generate conflict, stakes, or a decision.

---

# Design Philosophy

This stage follows one operating rule.

> **The audience does not follow an idea. The audience follows a person deciding.**

Every character exists to make the institutional tension visible through a human choice.

Characters that carry no goal, fear, or flaw are decorative and shall be removed.

---

# High-Level Flow

```text
dramatic-premise.json

logline.json

↓

Protagonist Design

↓

Supporting Role Design

↓

Character Arc Assignment

↓

(handoff to Conflict Engine)
```

---

# Inputs

```text
dramatic-premise.json

logline.json

theme.json
```

Author Style may constrain naming and terminology but shall never alter character function.

---

# Outputs

```text
character-bible.yaml
```

The character bible is immutable once approved.

---

# Character Cardinality

The Character Engine enforces strict cardinality.

| Role            | Count       | Rule                          |
| --------------- | ----------- | ----------------------------- |
| Protagonist     | Exactly 1   | Carries the dramatic question |
| Supporting      | 0 to 3      | Each must pressure the arc    |
| Antagonistic    | 0 to 1      | May be institutional, not human |

Exactly one protagonist must exist. Additional protagonists are prohibited.

Example

```yaml
protagonist:
  id: maya
  role: Enterprise Risk Director

supporting:
  - legal_director
  - ai_agent
```

---

# Protagonist Requirements

Every protagonist must contain the following fields.

```yaml
goal:

fear:

flaw:

competence:

decision_required:

arc:
```

Example

```yaml
goal:
  Stop the transaction.

fear:
  Losing institutional control.

flaw:
  Believes operational fixes are sufficient.

competence:
  Deep command of risk process.

decision_required:
  Redesign authority.

arc:
  Control
  →
  Understanding
  →
  Institutional Design
```

A protagonist missing any mandatory field is invalid.

---

# Supporting Role Requirements

Each supporting role must justify its presence.

```yaml
supporting_role:

  id:

  function:

  pressure_applied:

  relationship_to_protagonist:
```

Rules:

* Every supporting role must apply pressure on the protagonist's arc.
* A supporting role that only delivers exposition shall be removed.
* The antagonistic force may be institutional rather than a person.

---

# Character Arc Continuity

Each character arc must be continuous.

```text
Control

↓

Concern

↓

Disbelief

↓

Realization

↓

Determination
```

Arc stages may not be skipped. An arc that jumps from Control to Determination is invalid.

---

# Character-Premise Binding

Every character must trace back to the premise.

Rules:

* The protagonist's `decision_required` must equal the premise's required decision.
* The protagonist's `goal` must map to the logline goal.
* No character may exist without a function derived from the premise.

Unbound characters are rejected.

---

# Stage Contract

```python
class StoryStage:

    prepare()

    execute()

    validate()

    finalize()
```

`validate()` enforces cardinality, mandatory fields, and premise binding before emitting the character bible.

---

# Data Contracts

```text
character-bible.yaml

character-bible.meta.json

character.log
```

The decision log records why each supporting role was retained or removed.

---

# Failure Handling

```yaml
failures:

  multiple_protagonists:
    action: reject
    reason: "Exactly one protagonist is permitted."

  protagonist_missing_field:
    action: regenerate
    reason: "Mandatory protagonist field is absent."

  decorative_supporting_role:
    action: remove
    reason: "Supporting role applies no pressure."

  arc_discontinuity:
    action: regenerate
    reason: "Character arc skips a stage."
```

---

# Stage Quality Gate

```yaml
character_stage_gate:

  protagonist_count: 1

  supporting_role_count_max: 3

  mandatory_fields_present: true

  arc_continuous: true

```

Failure of any mandatory item blocks the Conflict Engine.

---

# Anti-Patterns

## AP-401 Ensemble Diffusion

Wrong

```text
Five equally weighted characters.
```

Correct

```text
One protagonist. Supporting roles apply pressure.
```

Diffused focus destroys the dramatic question.

## AP-402 Exposition Character

Wrong

```text
A character exists only to explain the concept.
```

Correct

```text

```

Section 2.1.4 is complete when:

* Exactly one protagonist is generated.
* Supporting roles number at most three and each applies pressure.
* Every protagonist contains all mandatory fields.
* Each character arc is continuous with no skipped stages.
* Every character is bound to the dramatic premise.
* Decorative and exposition-only characters are removed.
* The Conflict Engine depends only on the immutable character bible.

# 2.1.5 Conflict, Stakes & Time Pressure

## Insynergy Cinematic Thought Leadership Platform

### Master Specification v2.0

---

# Purpose

This section defines the stage that generates the forces acting on the protagonist: conflict, measurable stakes, and time pressure.

These three elements convert a character with a goal into a character under pressure.

A protagonist without opposition, without measurable loss, and without urgency cannot sustain a trailer.

---

# Design Philosophy

This stage follows one operating rule.

> **Drama is a decision made under pressure that cannot be postponed.**

Conflict supplies the opposition. Stakes supply the reason to care. Time pressure removes the option of waiting.

All three must be present simultaneously. The absence of any one collapses the drama.

---

# High-Level Flow

```text
character-bible.yaml

↓

Conflict Design

↓

Stakes Design

↓

Time Pressure Design

↓

(handoff to Narrative Structure)
```

---

# Inputs

```text
character-bible.yaml

dramatic-premise.json

theme.json
```

---

# Outputs

```text
conflict.json

stakes.json

time-pressure.json
```

Each artifact is immutable once approved.

---

# Conflict Engine

Every story must contain three layers of conflict.

```yaml
external:
  AI continues processing.

internal:
  Maya doubts her own authority.

institutional:
  Governance controls authority,
  but nobody designed it.
```

Rules:

* All three conflict layers must be present.
* The institutional conflict must derive from the primary theme.
* The internal conflict must derive from the protagonist's flaw.

---

# Conflict Layer Requirements

| Layer         | Source                       | Must Resolve Through           |
| ------------- | ---------------------------- | ------------------------------ |
| External      | Antagonistic force           | Protagonist action             |
| Internal      | Protagonist flaw             | Protagonist realization        |
| Institutional | Primary theme                | Protagonist decision (Act 3)   |

The institutional conflict is the conflict the trailer ultimately argues about.

---

# Stakes Engine

Every story requires measurable loss.

Allowed

* financial loss
* legal exposure
* operational failure
* reputational damage
* institutional collapse

Forbidden

* vague danger
* generic AI risk
* undefined consequences

Every stake must be expressed as a concrete, bounded loss.

---

# Stakes Quantification

Stakes shall be quantified wherever the source permits.

```json
{
  "stake_type": "legal_exposure",
  "measurable": true,
  "quantity": "legally binding contract",
  "reversible": false
}
```

# 2.1.8 Story Caching, Determinism & Interfaces

## Insynergy Cinematic Thought Leadership Platform

### Master Specification v2.0

# Purpose

This section defines the determinism and caching model of the Story Engine and enumerates its public interfaces.

Story generation is expensive and must never repeat unnecessarily.

Because story artifacts are deterministic, they are cacheable, and a cached story is reused whenever its inputs are unchanged.

---

# Design Philosophy

This stage follows one operating rule.

> **The best story generation is the one that never needs to run again.**

Determinism is the precondition for caching, reproducibility, and resume.

If the same inputs could produce different stories, none of these capabilities would be possible.

---

# Determinism Requirements

The Story Engine shall be deterministic along the canonical path.

```yaml
determinism:

  randomness: forbidden

  ordering: stable

  tie_breaking: declared

  provider_dependence: none
```

Given identical inputs, every stage shall produce byte-identical artifacts.

---

# Story Cache

Story artifacts are deterministic and therefore cacheable.

Cache Key

```text
sha256(

article_hash

+

story_engine_version

+

story_profile

)
```

If unchanged

Story Engine is skipped.

---

# Cache Key Composition

Each component of the cache key is defined explicitly.

| Component            | Meaning                                   |
| -------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| article_hash         | Hash of normalized article content        |
| story_engine_version | Version of the engine that produced output |
| story_profile        | Genre, audience, and duration configuration |

Any change to any component invalidates the cache entry.

---

# Cache Invalidation

```yaml
cache_invalidation:

  on_article_change: true

  on_engine_version_change: true

  on_profile_change: true

  on_author_style_change: true
```

Author Style participates in invalidation because it affects theme naming and terminology.

---

# Cache Lookup Flow

```text
Compute cache key

↓

Cache hit?

↓ yes → reuse artifacts

↓ no → run Story Engine → store artifacts
```

A cache hit bypasses every Story Engine stage.

# Purpose

# ADR Philosophy

The platform adopts the following principle.

> **Narrative decisions are long-lived; generation techniques are replaceable.**

* language models may change,

but narrative architectural intent must remain stable.

```text
Proposed

Accepted

↓

Implemented

↓

Superseded

↓

Archived
```

Only **Accepted** ADRs are normative.

---

# ADR Format

Every ADR shall contain the following fields.

```yaml
adr:

  id:

  title:

  status:

  date:

  context:

  decision:

  consequences:

  supersedes:

  superseded_by:
```

---

# ADR-201

## Story Precedes Rendering

### Status

Accepted

### Context

Cinematic rendering is expensive and probabilistic.

A weak story cannot be recovered by visual quality.

### Decision

No rendering may occur until the Story Quality Gate passes.

Story generation is a mandatory precondition for all downstream work.

### Consequences

Benefits

* wasted rendering cost is avoided
* weak narratives fail cheaply
* downstream stages receive validated input

Trade-offs

* stories may be regenerated multiple times before rendering begins

---

# ADR-202

## Exactly One Protagonist

### Status

Accepted

### Context

Trailers are short and cannot sustain divided focus.

Multiple protagonists dilute the dramatic question.

### Decision

Every story shall contain exactly one protagonist.

Supporting roles exist only to apply pressure.

### Consequences

Benefits

* a single, followable arc
* a clear dramatic question
* stable downstream character handling

Trade-offs

* ensemble source material must be reduced to one lead

---

# ADR-203

## One Dramatic Question

### Status

Accepted

### Context

A trailer resolves a single tension.

Multiple unresolved questions fragment the climax.

### Decision

Exactly one dramatic question shall exist and remain unresolved until Act 3.

### Consequences

Benefits

* a focused climax
* coherent stakes
* predictable act structure

Trade-offs

* secondary questions must be dropped or subordinated

---

# ADR-204

## Concept After Tension

### Status

Accepted

### Context

Institutional concepts are memorable only after the audience feels their absence.

A concept introduced first reads as a lecture.

### Decision

Concepts may appear only after dramatic tension exists, and must function as the answer to the dramatic question.

### Consequences

Benefits

* concepts land emotionally
* the trailer avoids lecturing
* concept ratio stays bounded

Trade-offs

* concept exposure is deferred, reducing explicit explanation time

---

# ADR-205

## Story Artifacts Are Immutable

### Status

Accepted

### Context

Downstream stages require stable, reproducible inputs.

Mutable story artifacts would break caching and resume.

### Decision

Story, character, and structure artifacts become immutable after approval.

Downstream engines consume them but never modify them.

### Consequences

Benefits

* deterministic cache
* reproducible builds
* resumable execution

Trade-offs

* changes require explicit regeneration rather than in-place edits

---

# ADR-206

## Deterministic Generation

### Status

Accepted

### Context

Caching and reproducibility require identical inputs to yield identical outputs.

### Decision

The canonical Story Engine path shall be deterministic. Randomness is forbidden and ties are broken by declared rules.

### Consequences

Benefits

* cacheable stories
* reproducible reviews
* trustworthy regression testing

Trade-offs

* creative variance must be introduced through inputs, not randomness

---

# ADR-207

## Downstream Depends Only on Artifacts

### Status

Accepted

### Context

Re-reading the original article downstream would reintroduce ambiguity the Story Engine exists to remove.

### Decision

Downstream modules shall depend only on story artifacts, never on the original article.

### Consequences

Benefits

* a single source of narrative truth
* stable interfaces
* isolation of the Story Engine

Trade-offs

* the Story Engine must capture everything downstream needs

---

# ADR-208

## The Quality Gate Cannot Be Silently Overridden

### Status

Accepted

### Context

A soft or bypassable gate would allow weak stories into expensive rendering.

### Decision

The Story Quality Gate is mandatory and all-or-nothing. Any exception requires a documented ADR.

### Consequences

Benefits

* consistent narrative quality
* auditable exceptions
* protected rendering budget

Trade-offs

* legitimate edge cases require formal ADR exceptions

---

# Acceptance Criteria

Section 2.1.9 is complete when:

* The ADR philosophy, lifecycle, and format are defined.
* Story-precedes-rendering is recorded as a normative decision.
* Single-protagonist and single-dramatic-question rules are recorded.
* Concept-after-tension and artifact immutability are recorded.

# Summary

The Story Engine converts an institutional idea into a single human decision made under irreversible pressure.

It produces a deterministic, cacheable, immutable set of artifacts, gated by a mandatory quality check.

Downstream production depends entirely on these artifacts, and never on the original article.

A trailer can only be as strong as the story beneath it — and this section defines how that story is guaranteed to be strong before a single frame is rendered.
