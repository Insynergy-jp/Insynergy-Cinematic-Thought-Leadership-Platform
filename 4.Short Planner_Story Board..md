# Purpose

This section defines the purpose, scope, authority, and architectural boundary of the **Shot Planner & Storyboard Engine**.

The Shot Planner transforms the screenplay into a production-ready visual shooting plan.

Its responsibility is **not** to generate video.

Its responsibility is to answer exactly one question:

> **"Exactly what must the camera capture?"**

The Storyboard Engine then converts the shot plan into deterministic visual specifications suitable for AI rendering.

Together, these two components form the **visual planning control plane** of the platform. They convert narrative intent into an explicit, testable, render-ready description of every frame the pipeline will later produce.

The Shot Planner & Storyboard Engine does not determine story meaning.

It expresses previously approved narrative meaning as concrete visual instructions.

---

# Governing Principle

The platform adopts the following principle.

> **The Shot Planner decomposes an approved screenplay into deterministic shots. It does not reinterpret the story.**

The Shot Planner may decide:

* how a scene is decomposed into shots;
* shot ordering within a scene;
* camera lens, angle, and movement;
* blocking and observable performance;
* storyboard composition and lighting intent;
* render strategy assignment.

The Shot Planner may not decide:

* the dramatic premise;
* which character is the protagonist;
* the stakes of the story;
* scene purpose or emotional progression;
* dialogue content;
* the three-act structure.

Visual decomposition is permitted.

Narrative reinterpretation is prohibited.

# Relationship to the Story Engine

The Story Engine defines:

> What the trailer means.

It establishes:

* the dramatic premise;
* the protagonist;
* the stakes;
* the three-act structure;
* the emotional arc.

The Shot Planner may not reopen any of these decisions. Story meaning is treated as immutable upstream intent.

---

# Relationship to the Screenplay Engine

The Screenplay Engine defines:

> What happens, scene by scene.

It produces:

* scene structure;
* scene purpose;
* dialogue;
* emotional progression;
* narration timing.

Section 4.1 consumes those screenplay artifacts and decomposes them into shots.

The Shot Planner shall not:

* rewrite dialogue;
* reorder scenes;
* modify scene purpose;
* alter emotional progression;
* analyze the original insight article.

If the screenplay is invalid or incomplete, the Shot Planner must reject it rather than silently infer missing narrative meaning.

---

# Relationship to Render Strategy & Runway Provider

The Render Strategy and Runway Provider layers define:

> How each shot is ultimately produced.

Section 4.1 assigns exactly one render strategy per shot but does not perform rendering.

The Shot Planner & Storyboard Engine shall not:

* submit jobs to Runway or any provider;
* download or compose rendered assets;
* select provider-specific rendering parameters;
* accept visually incorrect output to complete a build.

Downstream rendering depends only on Shot and Storyboard artifacts.

# Scope

The Shot Planner & Storyboard Engine is responsible for the following domains.

## Shot Decomposition

* consume screenplay scenes;
* decompose each scene into one or more shots;
* guarantee a single primary dramatic action per shot;
* assign a deterministic shot identity.

---

## Camera Planning

* define lens, angle, movement, and speed per shot;
* enforce a maximum of one camera movement per shot;
* record an explicit cinematic purpose for every shot.

---

## Blocking & Performance Direction

* describe only observable character movement;
* separate performance direction from dialogue;
* define emotional transition, eye focus, and breathing where relevant.

---

## Storyboard Generation

* express composition, lighting, palette, focal subject, and depth;
* describe visual intent without specifying technology.

---

## Continuity Management

* reference the Character Bible for every shot;
* reference the Location Bible for every shot;
* enforce forward-only time continuity.

---

## Render Strategy Assignment

* select exactly one render strategy per shot;
* enforce Runway eligibility and hybrid-rendering ratios;
* keep provider-specific decisions out of the storyboard.

---

## Quality Gating

* enforce the Shot Quality Gate;
* enforce the Storyboard Quality Gate;
* block invalid plans from reaching rendering.

# Principle — Scene Is Not a Video

A screenplay scene is **not** equal to one generated video.

```text
Scene

↓

One or More Shots

↓

Rendered Assets
```

A single scene may decompose into many shots, and each shot may be produced by a different render strategy. The mapping from narrative unit to rendered asset is deliberately many-staged so that expensive rendering is applied only where it is justified.

# Planning Boundary

The Shot Planner & Storyboard Engine is bounded by two contracts.

```text
Upstream Contract
Screenplay Artifacts
        │
        ▼
Shot Planner & Storyboard Engine
        │
        ▼
Downstream Contract
Shot & Storyboard Artifacts
```

The upstream contract defines what the story requires.

The downstream contract defines what must be captured and how it will be rendered.

The engine owns the transformation between the two but may not alter the semantic meaning of either.

---

# Upstream Contract

The engine requires the following immutable screenplay inputs.

```yaml

screenplay.json

character-bible.yaml

location-bible.yaml

camera-profile.yaml
```

The engine shall reject inputs that are missing, malformed, or inconsistent with one another rather than inferring missing narrative meaning.

---

# Downstream Contract

The engine produces the following artifacts.

```text
shot-list.json

storyboard.json

camera-plan.json

blocking.json

continuity-report.json

render-strategy.json
```

Every downstream artifact must be traceable to its originating screenplay scene, and downstream rendering shall depend only on these artifacts.

---

# Artifact Immutability

Once emitted and validated, Shot and Storyboard artifacts are immutable for the duration of a build.

The engine may attach planning metadata around the artifacts, including:

* shot cache key;
* quality-gate result;
* continuity-report reference;
* assigned render strategy.

The engine may not mutate an approved shot's dramatic action, camera definition, or continuity references after validation. A material change in inputs requires a new planning generation, not an in-place edit.

---

# Determinism Boundary

Repeated planning with identical inputs shall produce an identical shot plan.

Random decomposition decisions are prohibited. Given the same screenplay, character bible, location bible, camera profile, and planner version, the engine shall produce byte-stable `shot-list.json` and `storyboard.json` artifacts.

---

# Continuity Boundary

The engine references continuity; it does not redefine it.

* Character descriptions are owned by the Character Bible.
* Location descriptions are owned by the Location Bible.
* The planner references immutable identifiers and never duplicates character or location descriptions.
* Time may never move backward.

---

# Render Strategy Boundary

Each shot selects exactly one render strategy. No mixed strategies are permitted.

The engine enforces Runway eligibility and hybrid-rendering ratios but does not perform rendering, select provider parameters, or accept degraded output to complete a build.

---

# Human Approval Boundary

The engine must pause at the storyboard approval gate.

```text
Screenplay Approved
        │
        ▼
Shot Planning
        │
        ▼
Storyboard Generation
        │
        ▼
Story / Storyboard Approval
        │
        ▼
Render Strategy Execution
```

Approval is represented as a formal dependency, not an informal process note. No rendering may proceed until storyboard approval is recorded.

# Purpose

This section defines the **Shot Model** and the **Shot Schema**.

A Shot is the smallest visual unit that may be rendered independently.

Section 4.1.1 established *what* the Shot Planner must capture.

This section establishes the *unit* in which that capture is expressed.

The Shot Model gives every downstream component a single, shared definition of what a shot is.

The Shot Schema gives that model a concrete, testable, serialized form.

Everything the Shot Planner emits is composed of shots.

Every camera decision, blocking decision, emotional beat, continuity reference, and render assignment attaches to a shot.

The shot is therefore the atomic record of the entire visual planning control plane.

If the shot is malformed, nothing downstream can be correct.

This section defines the shot precisely enough that its correctness can be verified before any other planning work begins.

---

# Design Philosophy

The platform adopts the following principle.

> **A Shot is the smallest visual unit that may be rendered independently. Each shot contains only one primary dramatic action.**

This principle has two halves, and both are load-bearing.

The first half — *smallest independently-renderable unit* — defines the shot's **granularity**.

The second half — *one primary dramatic action* — defines the shot's **semantic content**.

A record that violates either half is not a shot.

The Shot Model exists so that expensive, non-deterministic rendering is applied to the smallest possible well-defined unit.

Smaller units are cheaper to re-render, cheaper to cache, and cheaper to reject.

A single dramatic action per unit is what makes a shot legible to a camera, to a performer, and to a render provider.

Granularity without semantic focus produces fragments.

Semantic focus without granularity produces scenes.

The shot is the deliberate intersection of the two.

---

# What Makes a Shot Independently Renderable

A shot is *independently renderable* when it can be produced without reference to any other shot.

This is a property of the shot's completeness, not its position.

For a shot to be independently renderable, it must carry — by value or by reference — everything a render strategy needs to produce it.

```text
Shot
  ├── who is on screen        (subject)
  ├── where it happens        (location)
  ├── how the camera sees it  (camera)
  ├── what the subject does   (blocking)
  ├── what the subject feels  (emotion)
  ├── what must stay stable   (continuity)
  └── how it will be produced (render_strategy)
```

A shot that requires the previous shot's camera state to be understood is **not** independently renderable.

A shot that inherits its subject implicitly from a neighbor is **not** independently renderable.

Each shot is self-describing.

Independence is what allows the pipeline to render shots in any order, in parallel, on different providers, and to re-render exactly one shot without rebuilding the scene.

Independence is also what makes the shot the correct unit of caching, as defined in 4.1.13.

> **Independence is the boundary. If a unit cannot be rendered alone, it is not a shot.**

---

# The Shot Schema

Every shot is represented by the following canonical schema.

```json
{
  "shot_id": "scene-004-shot-02",
  "scene_id": "scene-004",
  "act": 2,
  "duration_seconds": 4,
  "shot_type": "medium_close_up",
  "purpose": "Reveal emotional realization",
  "subject": "MAYA",
  "location": "Operations Center",
  "camera": {},
  "blocking": {},
  "emotion": {},
  "continuity": {},
  "render_strategy": "runway_video"
}
```

This object is the atomic record of the Shot Planner.

Every field is mandatory.

The scalar fields — `shot_id`, `scene_id`, `act`, `duration_seconds`, `shot_type`, `purpose`, `subject`, `location`, and `render_strategy` — are populated by the Shot Model at decomposition time.

The nested objects — `camera`, `blocking`, `emotion`, and `continuity` — are declared here as empty structures and populated by later sections.

An empty nested object is a valid initial state.

A missing nested object is a schema violation.

Each field has a defined meaning, type, and constraint.

| Field              | Type    | Meaning                                             | Constraint                                            |
| ------------------ | ------- | --------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| `shot_id`          | string  | Globally unique identity of the shot                | Must match `scene-NNN-shot-NN`; unique across build  |

| `act`              | integer | Act in the three-act structure the shot belongs to  | One of 1, 2, or 3                                     |
| `duration_seconds` | integer | Intended on-screen duration of the shot             | Positive integer, in seconds                         |
| `shot_type`        | string  | Cinematic framing category (see 4.1.3)              | Must be a valid shot type                            |
| `purpose`          | string  | The single cinematic reason the shot exists         | Non-empty; one purpose per shot                      |
| `subject`          | string  | The primary on-screen subject                       | A Character Bible identifier                         |
| `location`         | string  | The place the shot occurs                           | A Location Bible identifier                          |
| `camera`           | object  | Camera language for the shot (see 4.1.4)            | Object; populated by 4.1.4                           |
| `blocking`         | object  | Observable performance and movement (see 4.1.5)    | Object; populated by 4.1.5                           |
| `emotion`          | object  | Emotional transition of the subject (see 4.1.5)    | Object; populated by 4.1.5                           |
| `continuity`       | object  | Continuity references (see 4.1.7)                  | Object; populated by 4.1.7                           |

# Field Semantics — Scalars

Each scalar field carries a distinct responsibility.

## shot_id

The `shot_id` is the shot's identity.

In the example it is `"scene-004-shot-02"`.

It is globally unique within a build and never reused.

It is the key by which every other artifact — camera plan, blocking, continuity report, render strategy — refers to this shot.

## scene_id

The `scene_id` binds the shot to exactly one screenplay scene.

In the example it is `"scene-004"`.

This satisfies Invariant P-401 from 4.1.1: every shot maps to exactly one screenplay scene.

The `scene_id` is always the prefix of the `shot_id`.

## act

The `act` records which act of the three-act structure the shot belongs to.

In the example it is `2`.

The act is inherited from the screenplay and may never be reassigned by the Shot Planner.

## duration_seconds

The `duration_seconds` field records the intended on-screen duration.

In the example it is `4`.

Duration is a planning intent, not a rendered fact; the render layer honors it but does not set it.

## shot_type

The `shot_type` classifies the framing.

In the example it is `"medium_close_up"`.

The catalog of valid shot types and their cinematic meaning is defined in 4.1.3.

## purpose

The `purpose` states the single cinematic reason the shot exists.

In the example it is `"Reveal emotional realization"`.

This field satisfies Invariant P-404: every shot has an explicit cinematic purpose.

A shot with no purpose is rejected.

## subject

The `subject` names the primary on-screen subject.

In the example it is `"MAYA"`.

It is a Character Bible identifier, not a free-text description.

## location

The `location` names where the shot occurs.

In the example it is `"Operations Center"`.

It is a Location Bible identifier.

## render_strategy

The `render_strategy` names the single strategy that will produce the shot.

In the example it is `"runway_video"`.

Its semantics and the eligibility rules that govern it are defined in 4.1.9.

# The One Primary Dramatic Action Rule

The governing constraint of the Shot Model is stated once and enforced everywhere.

> **Each shot contains only one primary dramatic action.**

A dramatic action is the single observable beat the shot exists to deliver.

In the reference shot, the action is captured by `purpose` = `"Reveal emotional realization"`.

That is one action: a realization is revealed.

The rule forbids composite shots.

A shot that reveals a realization *and* introduces a new character *and* relocates the subject is three shots, not one.

The correct response to a multi-action beat is decomposition into multiple shots, each with its own `shot_id`.

This rule is what keeps a shot legible.

A camera can frame one action.

A performer can perform one action.

A render provider can produce one action deterministically.

Two primary actions in one unit make all three ambiguous.

The rule is formalized as Invariant P-422 below and mirrors Invariant P-402 from 4.1.1.

Wrong:

```text
shot reveals realization
        +
shot also introduces a new subject
```

Correct:

```text
shot-02  reveals realization   (subject: MAYA)
        │
        ▼
shot-03  introduces new subject
```

# Shot Identity & Naming

Every shot carries a deterministic identity.

The identity format is fixed.

```text
scene-NNN-shot-NN
```

* `scene-NNN` is the zero-padded originating scene number.
* `shot-NN` is the zero-padded ordinal of the shot within that scene.

In the reference example:

```text
scene-004-shot-02
   │          │
   │          └── the second shot of the scene
   └───────────── originating scene 004
```

The `scene_id` (`"scene-004"`) is always the exact prefix of the `shot_id` (`"scene-004-shot-02"`).

This relationship is not decorative; it is checkable.

```python
assert shot["shot_id"].startswith(shot["scene_id"] + "-shot-")
```

Naming rules:

* identity is deterministic — identical inputs produce identical shot ids;
* identity is stable — a shot id is never reassigned within a build;
* identity is unique — no two shots share a shot id;
* ordinals are contiguous within a scene and reflect shot order.

Deterministic naming is what makes the shot plan reproducible, as required by the determinism boundary of 4.1.1.

# Nested Objects & Deferred Population

The Shot Schema declares four nested objects as empty at decomposition time.

```json
"camera": {},
"blocking": {},
"emotion": {},
"continuity": {}
```

Each empty object is a **contract slot**.

The Shot Model owns the slot; a later section owns its contents.

| Nested Object | Populated By | Section | Content                                          |
| ------------- | ------------ | ------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| `camera`      | Camera Planner       | 4.1.4 | lens, angle, movement, speed                     |
| `blocking`    | Blocking Planner     | 4.1.5 | observable character movement and position       |
| `emotion`     | Blocking Planner     | 4.1.5 | emotional transition, eye focus, breathing       |
| `continuity`  | Continuity Validator | 4.1.7 | Character Bible and Location Bible references     |

The population order is strict.

Decomposition (this section) emits the scalar fields and the empty slots.

4.1.4 fills `camera`.

4.1.5 fills `blocking` and `emotion`.

4.1.7 fills `continuity`.

A shot is not render-ready until all four slots are populated and validated.

> **The Shot Model defines the slots. Later sections fill them. No section fills a slot it does not own.**

An empty slot is a valid intermediate state.

An absent slot is a malformed shot.

---

# Population Lifecycle

A shot moves through a defined sequence of states as its slots are filled.

```text
Decomposed
   │  (scalars set, nested objects empty)
   ▼
Camera-Planned          4.1.4  → camera{}
   │
   ▼
Performance-Directed    4.1.5  → blocking{}, emotion{}
   │
   ▼
Continuity-Validated    4.1.7  → continuity{}
   │
   ▼
Render-Assigned         4.1.9  → render_strategy
   │
   ▼
Render-Ready
```

The scalar `render_strategy` is set at decomposition with a provisional value and finalized by 4.1.9.

In the reference shot its value is `"runway_video"`.

Each transition is additive.

No transition rewrites a scalar field set at decomposition.

The `shot_id`, `scene_id`, `act`, `duration_seconds`, `shot_type`, `purpose`, `subject`, and `location` are fixed at decomposition and immutable thereafter, consistent with the artifact immutability boundary of 4.1.1.

# Render Strategy Linkage

The `render_strategy` field links each shot to the render layer.

# 4.1.3 — Shot Types & Cinematic Purpose

## Insynergy Cinematic Thought Leadership Platform

### Master Specification v2.0

---

# Purpose

This section defines the **controlled vocabulary of shot types** available to the Shot Planner and binds every shot to an explicit cinematic purpose.

Section 4.1.2 established *what a shot is*. This section establishes *what kind of shot it may be* and *why that kind was chosen*.

A shot type is not a stylistic flourish. It is a declaration of what the camera captures and how the viewer is positioned relative to the dramatic action.

The Shot Planner answers one question here:

> **"Which shot type serves this dramatic action, and what is that shot type's cinematic purpose?"**

The engine restricts shot types to a closed enumeration. It forbids unlabeled or arbitrary framing. It requires that the reason for each shot type be recorded, testable, and traceable to the screenplay scene it serves.

This section does not choose the story beat. It expresses an already-approved beat as a deliberate, bounded visual choice.

---

# Design Philosophy

The platform treats shot-type selection as an engineering decision, not an aesthetic accident.

> **A shot type is a contract between the dramatic action and the viewer's eye. Every shot type carries a cinematic purpose, and no shot may exist without one.**

Three commitments follow from this philosophy.

* **Closed vocabulary.** Shot types are drawn from a fixed enumeration. The planner may not invent framing outside the list.
* **Explicit purpose.** Each shot records the cinematic reason its type was selected. Purpose is data, not narration.
* **Deterministic mapping.** Given the same dramatic action and the same scene intent, the planner selects the same shot type.

Cinematic craft is preserved by constraint, not by freedom. A small, well-understood vocabulary applied with intent produces a coherent visual language. An open vocabulary applied without intent produces noise.

---

# Governing Principle

The platform adopts the following principle without exception.

> **Every shot must have an explicit cinematic purpose.**

This is not advisory. It is the governing law of Section 4.1.3.

A shot without a declared purpose is an invalid shot. It does not describe a defect in taste; it describes a defect in the plan. Such a shot must be rejected by the Shot Quality Gate before it can reach the Storyboard Engine.

This principle is the local expression of the platform-wide **Invariant P-404** established in Section 4.1.1:

> **Every shot has an explicit cinematic purpose.**

Section 4.1.3 refines P-404 from a boundary invariant into an enforceable typing rule: purpose is bound to shot type, and the pairing is validated as a unit.

---

# The Allowed Shot Types

The Shot Planner may select a shot type only from the following closed enumeration.

```yaml
establishing

wide

medium

medium_close_up

close_up

extreme_close_up

over_shoulder

point_of_view

insert

tracking

static
```

These eleven values are the complete and exclusive vocabulary of shot types.

No other value is valid. The enumeration is exhaustive for the platform. A shot whose type is absent from this list fails typing before any further planning proceeds.

The enumeration is case-sensitive and stable. Values are recorded exactly as written above, including underscores. The planner shall not alias, abbreviate, pluralize, or re-case any value.

---

# The Forbidden Shot Type

The following value is explicitly forbidden.

```yaml
random
```

`random` is not a shot type. It is the absence of a decision.

The platform forbids `random` because it directly contradicts two foundational commitments:

* the **Determinism Boundary** of Section 4.1.1, which requires that identical inputs produce an identical shot plan;
* the **Governing Principle** of Section 4.1.3, which requires that every shot carry an explicit cinematic purpose.

A `random` shot type asserts that framing was left to chance. A framing left to chance has no cinematic purpose. A shot with no cinematic purpose violates P-404.

Therefore `random` can never be reconciled with the specification. It is rejected at typing time, not merely discouraged.

> **Randomness is the opposite of intent. The platform plans shots; it does not gamble them.**

---

# Shot Type Definitions

Each allowed shot type carries a defined meaning and a typical narrative use. The definitions below are the reference semantics the planner applies when selecting a type.

---

## establishing

The `establishing` shot situates the viewer in space, time, and context before the action begins.

* **What it captures:** the whole setting — location, scale, and the relationship of subjects to their environment.
* **Typical narrative use:** opening a scene, orienting the audience, signaling a change of place, or grounding an abstract idea in a concrete world.

The `establishing` shot answers "where are we?" before the story asks anything else of the viewer.

---

## wide

The `wide` shot frames the full subject and a substantial portion of the surrounding space.

* **What it captures:** the subject in full, with room to read body language and spatial relationships.
* **Typical narrative use:** blocking multiple characters, showing physical action, or holding a subject inside a meaningful environment.

Where `establishing` prioritizes place, `wide` prioritizes the subject within place.

---

## medium

The `medium` shot frames a subject from roughly the waist up.

* **What it captures:** a balance of the subject and enough surrounding context to read gesture and posture.
* **Typical narrative use:** dialogue, presentation, and the neutral workhorse framing of conversational scenes.

The `medium` shot is the baseline register — close enough for connection, wide enough for context.

---

## medium_close_up

The `medium_close_up` frames a subject from roughly the chest or shoulders up.

* **What it captures:** the face and upper body, tightening focus on expression while retaining minimal context.
* **Typical narrative use:** intensifying a conversation, marking a shift toward the personal, or emphasizing a speaker's conviction.

It is the deliberate step inward from `medium` toward emotional proximity.

---

## close_up

The `close_up` fills the frame with the subject's face or a single significant detail.

* **What it captures:** expression, reaction, and emotional truth at close range.
* **Typical narrative use:** revealing feeling, punctuating a dramatic beat, or forcing the viewer to confront a decisive moment.

The `close_up` collapses distance. It tells the viewer that *this* is what matters now.

---

## extreme_close_up

The `extreme_close_up` isolates a fragment of the subject — an eye, a mouth, a hand, a single object detail.

* **What it captures:** magnified detail that the naked eye would overlook.
* **Typical narrative use:** heightening tension, revealing a critical detail, or rendering an internal state with visceral intensity.

It is the most concentrated framing in the vocabulary. It magnifies significance by eliminating everything else.

---

## over_shoulder

The `over_shoulder` frames one subject from behind and to the side of another.

* **What it captures:** the relationship between two subjects, anchoring the viewer's position within an exchange.
* **Typical narrative use:** dialogue, confrontation, and any moment where the geometry of a relationship carries meaning.

The `over_shoulder` shot binds two subjects into a single spatial contract of attention.

---

## point_of_view

The `point_of_view` shot presents what a subject sees, from that subject's vantage.

* **What it captures:** the scene as experienced through a character's eyes.
* **Typical narrative use:** aligning the viewer with a character's perspective, building empathy, or revealing a discovery as the character makes it.

The `point_of_view` shot converts observation into identification.

---

## insert

The `insert` isolates a specific object or detail that the narrative requires the viewer to notice.

* **What it captures:** a discrete element — a document, a screen, a hand on an object — cut into the flow of a scene.
* **Typical narrative use:** directing attention to information, emphasizing a prop, or supplying a detail the wider framing cannot show.

The `insert` is informational. It exists to make one specific thing legible.

---

## tracking

The `tracking` shot moves with a subject or through a space.

* **What it captures:** motion — the camera following, leading, or accompanying its subject.
* **Typical narrative use:** sustaining momentum, revealing a space progressively, or binding the viewer to a subject in motion.

The `tracking` shot is the platform's principal expression of deliberate camera movement, and it remains bound by the one-movement-per-shot rule of Invariant P-403.

---

## static

The `static` shot holds a fixed frame with no camera movement.

* **What it captures:** a stable, composed image in which any motion belongs to the subject, not the camera.
* **Typical narrative use:** contemplation, gravity, precision, or letting a performance carry a moment without camera intervention.

The `static` shot is the deliberate absence of movement. Stillness is a choice, and here it is a recorded one.

---

# Shot Type → Dramatic Function

The following table maps each allowed shot type to its typical dramatic function. It is the reference the planner and the Quality Gate use to test whether a selected type serves its declared purpose.

| Shot Type          | Typical Dramatic Function                                             |
| ------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| `establishing`     | Orient the viewer in space, time, and context.                       |
| `wide`             | Present the full subject within a meaningful environment.            |
| `medium`           | Sustain conversation and neutral, balanced presentation.             |
| `medium_close_up`  | Tighten toward the personal and intensify a speaker's conviction.    |
| `close_up`         | Reveal emotion and punctuate a decisive dramatic beat.               |
| `extreme_close_up` | Magnify a critical detail and heighten tension.                      |
| `over_shoulder`    | Anchor the geometry of a relationship or exchange.                   |
| `point_of_view`    | Align the viewer with a character's perspective.                     |
| `insert`           | Make a specific object or piece of information legible.              |
| `tracking`         | Carry momentum and reveal space through motion.                      |
| `static`           | Hold stillness for contemplation, gravity, or precision.            |

The mapping is a guide to typical function, not a straitjacket. A shot type may serve a purpose beyond its typical function when the declared cinematic purpose justifies it. What the planner may never do is select a type with no declared purpose at all.

---

# Cinematic Purpose Is Mandatory Data

Cinematic purpose is a required, structured field on every shot. It is not narration and it is not optional.

```yaml
shot:
  shot_type: close_up
  cinematic_purpose: "Reveal the protagonist's decision at the moment of commitment."
```

The purpose field records *why this shot type was selected for this dramatic action*.

The engine enforces the following rules on cinematic purpose.

* Purpose must be present on every shot.
* Purpose must be non-empty.
* Purpose must reference the dramatic action the shot serves.
* Purpose must be consistent with the selected shot type.

A shot type without a purpose, or a purpose that contradicts the selected type, is invalid. The pairing of `shot_type` and `cinematic_purpose` is validated as a single unit.

# Relationship to the Visual Grammar (4.1.6)

Shot-type selection in 4.1.3 is the upstream input to the **Visual Grammar** defined in Section 4.1.6.

Section 4.1.3 decides *what kind of shot* and *why*. Section 4.1.6 decides *how that shot is composed* — framing, composition, lighting intent, palette, focal subject, and depth.

```text
4.1.3 Shot Type + Cinematic Purpose
        │
        ▼
4.1.6 Visual Grammar
(Composition · Lighting · Palette · Focal Subject · Depth)
        │
        ▼
Storyboard Specification
```

The relationship is directional and non-negotiable.

* The shot type constrains the Visual Grammar. A `close_up` demands a composition consistent with a `close_up`.
* The cinematic purpose informs the Visual Grammar. A purpose of "heighten tension" shapes lighting and palette intent downstream.
* The Visual Grammar may not override the shot type. It expresses the shot type; it does not replace it.

A shot type is the grammatical category. The Visual Grammar is the sentence built within that category. The category is chosen first, and it is chosen here.

# 4.1.4 — Camera Language & Constraints

## Insynergy Cinematic Thought Leadership Platform

### Master Specification v2.0

---

# Purpose

This section defines the **camera language** and the **hard camera constraints** that govern every shot produced by the Shot Planner.

The camera language is the controlled vocabulary the planner uses to describe how a shot is seen.

Every shot defines camera properties. No shot is complete without them.

The Shot Planner does not improvise camera behavior.

It selects from a fixed, enumerated set of lenses, angles, movements, and speeds, and it obeys explicit numeric limits on complexity.

This section answers exactly one question:

> **"With what optical grammar, and within what limits, may the camera describe a shot?"**

The camera language exists so that visual intent is deterministic, testable, and render-ready.

A shot that cannot be expressed in this vocabulary is not a valid shot.

---

# Design Philosophy

The platform treats camera description as a **constrained language**, not a free-form artistic note.

> **A cinematic language is powerful because it is small. Constraint is what makes a shot legible, deterministic, and renderable.**

Three principles drive the design.

## Enumerated, Not Open-Ended

Camera properties are chosen from closed enumerations.

The planner may not invent a lens, an angle, or a movement that is not listed.

An unlisted value is a validation failure, never a creative liberty.

---

## Simplicity Is Enforced, Not Encouraged

Complexity is bounded by explicit numeric maximums.

The planner may not stack subjects, movements, actions, or emotional transitions beyond the declared limits.

A shot that tries to say too much says nothing clearly, and it fails validation.

---

## Deterministic Over Expressive

Given identical inputs, the planner emits an identical camera plan.

The camera language contains no ambiguous, subjective, or provider-specific terms.

Every value in the vocabulary maps to a stable, machine-checkable meaning.

---

# The Camera Object

Every shot defines camera properties.

The camera object is the canonical expression of Section 4.9 — Camera Language.

```yaml
camera:

  lens:
    24mm
    35mm
    50mm
    85mm

  angle:
    eye
    low
    high

  movement:
    static
    slow_push
    slow_pull
    dolly
    pan
    tilt

  speed:
    slow
```

Every field is drawn from these enumerations and nothing else.

The camera object carries four properties: `lens`, `angle`, `movement`, and `speed`.

> **Maximum one movement.**

A shot declares at most one camera movement. This rule is absolute and is formalized as an Invariant below.

---

# Lens Vocabulary

The `lens` field selects the focal length. Four lenses are available.

Each lens carries a distinct cinematic effect and is chosen for intent, not convenience.

```text
24mm  →  wide, environmental, expansive
35mm  →  natural-wide, grounded, documentary
50mm  →  natural, human, neutral
85mm  →  intimate, compressed, portrait
```

## 24mm

The `24mm` lens is the wide lens.

It captures environment, scale, and context.

It expands space, establishes place, and situates a subject inside a larger world.

Use it to open a scene, to reveal setting, or to make a subject feel small against its surroundings.

Wide focal length exaggerates depth and distance; near objects loom and far objects recede.

---

## 35mm

The `35mm` lens is the natural-wide lens.

It is grounded and documentary in feel.

It keeps environment legible while staying close to how the eye perceives a room.

Use it when the shot must hold both subject and context without the exaggeration of `24mm`.

It is the workhorse for grounded, credible framing.

---

## 50mm

The `50mm` lens is the natural lens.

It approximates human vision and introduces no perspective distortion.

It renders a subject as the eye would see it — neutral, honest, and present.

Use it for direct, unstyled framing where the subject, not the optics, carries the meaning.

It is the default when no stronger optical statement is required.

---

## 85mm

The `85mm` lens is the intimate lens.

It compresses space and isolates the subject from the background.

It flattens depth, softens what lies behind the focal plane, and draws the viewer close.

Use it for portraiture, emotional proximity, and moments of interior focus.

Long focal length is the language of intimacy and attention.

---

# Angle Vocabulary

The `angle` field selects the vertical camera position relative to the subject. Three angles are available.

```text
eye   →  neutral, equal, honest
low   →  power, dominance, scale
high  →  vulnerability, diminishment, oversight
```

## eye

The `eye` angle places the camera level with the subject.

It is neutral, equal, and honest.

It neither elevates nor diminishes; it presents the subject as a peer to the viewer.

Use it as the default for balanced, trustworthy framing.

---

## low

The `low` angle places the camera below the subject, looking up.

It confers power, dominance, and scale.

The subject grows against the frame and asserts presence.

Use it when a subject, idea, or moment must feel commanding or monumental.

---

## high

The `high` angle places the camera above the subject, looking down.

It conveys vulnerability, diminishment, and oversight.

The subject shrinks within the frame and yields presence.

Use it when a subject must feel exposed, small, or observed.

---

# Movement Vocabulary

The `movement` field selects a single camera motion. Six movement options are available.

```text
static      →  no motion; stillness and stability
slow_push   →  gradual move toward the subject
slow_pull   →  gradual move away from the subject
dolly       →  lateral or tracking travel
pan         →  horizontal rotation on a fixed axis
tilt        →  vertical rotation on a fixed axis
```

## static

`static` declares no camera motion.

The frame holds still.

Stillness conveys stability, observation, and composure. It lets performance and composition carry the shot.

---

## slow_push

`slow_push` moves the camera gradually toward the subject.

It increases intimacy and intensifies focus over the duration of the shot.

Use it to draw the viewer inward as a moment builds.

---

## slow_pull

`slow_pull` moves the camera gradually away from the subject.

It reveals context, releases tension, or isolates the subject within a widening frame.

Use it to open outward as a moment resolves.

---

## dolly

`dolly` travels the camera laterally or tracks alongside the subject.

It introduces parallax and a sense of physical movement through space.

Use it to accompany motion or to sweep across an environment.

---

## pan

`pan` rotates the camera horizontally on a fixed axis.

It redirects attention across the horizontal plane without relocating the camera.

Use it to shift from one subject or region of the frame to another.

---

## tilt

`tilt` rotates the camera vertically on a fixed axis.

It redirects attention across the vertical plane without relocating the camera.

Use it to reveal height, scale, or a vertical relationship between subjects.

---

# The One-Movement Rule

A shot declares **at most one** camera movement.

> **Maximum one movement.**

The planner may not combine movements. A shot may not both `pan` and `tilt`, nor `slow_push` while it `dolly`s.

If a shot appears to require two movements, it is two shots.

This rule keeps every shot legible, deterministic, and renderable, and it is enforced as Invariant **P-461** below.

`static` is itself a valid selection of the `movement` field; declaring `static` satisfies the rule and expresses a deliberate absence of motion.

---

# Speed Vocabulary

The `speed` field qualifies the pace of the camera.

```yaml
speed:
  slow
```

The camera language defines the value `slow`.

Speed describes deliberate, measured motion consistent with the platform's cinematic, thought-leadership register.

The camera moves with intent, never with haste.

---

# Hard Camera Constraints

Beyond the vocabulary, every shot is bounded by explicit numeric limits.

These are the canonical constraints of Section 4.20 — Camera Constraints.

The maximum values are:

```yaml
primary_subjects: 2

camera_movements: 1

primary_actions: 1

emotional_transitions: 1
```

> **Violations fail validation.**

Each limit is a ceiling, not a target. A shot may declare fewer, never more.

---

## primary_subjects — maximum 2

A shot may frame at most **2** primary subjects.

Two subjects allow relationship — a contrast, an exchange, a confrontation — without crowding the frame.

A third primary subject fractures attention and is rejected.

---

## camera_movements — maximum 1

A shot may declare at most **1** camera movement.

This is the numeric expression of the one-movement rule and of the camera vocabulary's `movement` field.

A second movement is rejected.

---

## primary_actions — maximum 1

A shot may contain at most **1** primary action.

One shot expresses one dramatic action. This aligns with the single-primary-action guarantee of the Shot Planner.

A second primary action means the shot must be split.

---

## emotional_transitions — maximum 1

A shot may contain at most **1** emotional transition.

A single shot may carry a subject from one emotional state to one other, and no further.

A second emotional transition is rejected.

---

# Constraint Summary Table

The complete numeric contract for a valid camera plan.

| Property                | Maximum | Meaning                                            |
| ----------------------- | ------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| `primary_subjects`      | 2       | At most two primary subjects share the frame.      |
| `camera_movements`      | 1       | At most one camera movement per shot.              |
| `primary_actions`       | 1       | At most one primary dramatic action per shot.      |
| `emotional_transitions` | 1       | At most one emotional transition per shot.         |

Every value is a hard ceiling. Violations fail validation.

---

# Enforcement — The Shot Quality Gate

Camera constraints are not advisory.

They are enforced at validation time by the Shot Quality Gate defined in Section 4.1.12.

```text
Shot Camera Plan
        │
        ▼
Validate Enumerations (lens, angle, movement, speed)
        │
        ▼
Validate Numeric Constraints
   primary_subjects   ≤ 2
   camera_movements   ≤ 1
   primary_actions    ≤ 1
   emotional_transitions ≤ 1
        │
        ▼
Pass → Render-Ready Shot
Fail → Rejected Shot
```

A shot that exceeds any maximum, or that uses a value outside the enumerated vocabulary, does not pass the gate.

A rejected shot never reaches storyboard generation or rendering.

The gate fails closed: an unvalidated camera plan is treated as invalid.

# 4.1.5 — Blocking & Performance Direction

## Insynergy Cinematic Thought Leadership Platform

### Master Specification v2.0

---

# Purpose

This section defines how the Shot Planner describes **blocking** and **performance direction** for every shot.

Blocking specifies how characters move.

Performance direction specifies how characters perform.

These are two distinct concerns, and the platform keeps them distinct.

Blocking answers one question:

> **"What movement must the camera observe?"**

Performance direction answers a different question:

> **"What emotional state must the frame express?"**

Neither concern generates video.

Neither concern rewrites dialogue.

Both concerns produce explicit, testable fields that populate the shot schema defined in Section 4.1.2.

The result is a shot whose physical action and emotional intent are declared separately, unambiguously, and in filmable terms.

---

# Design Philosophy

The platform adopts a single discipline for this section.

> **Only observable movement.**

A shot plan is a set of instructions to a camera and a renderer, not a novelist's interior monologue.

If a field cannot be seen in the frame, it does not belong in blocking.

Blocking therefore records physical, observable action and nothing else.

Performance direction records emotional intent, but it is deliberately **separated from dialogue** so that the emotion of a moment is never confused with the words spoken in it.

This separation produces three properties the platform depends on:

* blocking is renderable, because every field is a visible movement;
* performance is testable, because every field is an explicit named state;
* dialogue is protected, because performance direction never rewrites what is said.

The engine expresses previously approved narrative meaning as concrete visual and emotional instructions. It does not invent new meaning.

---

# The Two Concerns

Blocking and performance are complementary but never merged.

```text
Character
    │
    ├─► Blocking ──────► Observable Movement
    │
    └─► Performance ───► Emotional Intent
```

Blocking is what the body does.

Performance is what the interior state is, expressed only through what the frame can show.

The Shot Planner defines both, keeps them in separate objects, and never lets one silently absorb the other.

---

# Blocking

Blocking specifies how characters move.

It is decomposed into three observable phases.

```text
start ──► action ──► end
```

Each phase is a physical state or a physical movement.

Each phase is something the camera can literally capture.

Blocking never describes what a character is thinking, feeling, remembering, or intending. It describes only the movement.

> **Only observable movement.**

---

# Blocking Structure

Blocking is expressed as a `start`, an `action`, and an `end`.

```yaml
MAYA:

  start:
    standing

  action:
    reach_for_override

  end:
    frozen
```

The three fields carry a strict meaning.

* `start` — the observable physical state at the beginning of the shot.
* `action` — the single observable movement performed during the shot.
* `end` — the observable physical state at the end of the shot.

In the canonical example, the character **MAYA** begins `standing`, performs the action `reach_for_override`, and ends `frozen`.

The movement is linear and filmable: a person who is upright reaches for a control, and then stops.

Nothing in the blocking states why MAYA freezes. That is not blocking's concern.

---

# The MAYA Example

The MAYA example is the reference blocking for this section.

```text
standing  →  reach_for_override  →  frozen
```

Read the three phases as a camera would see them.

| Phase    | Field                | What the camera captures                     |
| -------- | -------------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| start    | `standing`           | MAYA is upright and still.                   |
| action   | `reach_for_override` | MAYA extends toward the override control.    |
| end      | `frozen`             | MAYA halts mid-gesture and holds position.   |

Every value in this table is a visible fact.

`standing` is a posture.

`reach_for_override` is a movement toward a physical control.

`frozen` is the cessation of that movement.

There is no interior content anywhere in the blocking. The reason MAYA freezes belongs to performance direction, not to blocking.

---

# Observable Movement Only

Blocking admits only what can be filmed.

The following are permitted, because a camera can capture them:

* posture — `standing`;
* directed movement — `reach_for_override`;
* cessation of movement — `frozen`.

The following are prohibited in blocking, because a camera cannot capture them:

* interior thought;
* remembered events;
* unspoken intention;
* motivation;
* feeling that produces no visible movement.

> **Only observable movement.**

If a proposed blocking field cannot be pointed to in the frame, it is not blocking. It is either performance direction or narrative meaning, and it must be moved to its proper place or rejected.

---

# Performance Direction

Performance is explicitly defined.

Where blocking records the body, performance direction records the emotional intent of the moment — expressed in named, bounded fields.

```yaml
emotion:

  before:
    controlled

  after:
    disbelief

eyes:

  focus:
    countdown

breathing:

  restrained
```

Performance direction is explicit rather than implied. The emotional state is not left for a downstream renderer to guess; it is declared.

---

# Performance Direction Fields

Performance direction is composed of three field groups.

* `emotion` — the emotional transition across the shot, expressed as `before` and `after`.
* `eyes` — where the character's attention is directed, expressed as `focus`.
* `breathing` — the observable breath state of the character.

Each field is an explicit named value.

Each field expresses interior state only through what the frame can carry: an expression, a gaze, the rise and fall of breath.

---

# Emotion — Before and After

The `emotion` group declares an emotional transition.

```yaml
emotion:

  before:
    controlled

  after:
    disbelief
```

The transition has two anchors.

* `before` — the emotional state entering the shot. In the reference, `controlled`.
* `after` — the emotional state leaving the shot. In the reference, `disbelief`.

The shot therefore carries a movement from `controlled` to `disbelief`.

This is an emotional arc across a single shot, declared explicitly, so that the performance is testable and repeatable rather than improvised.

---

# Eyes — Focus

The `eyes` group declares where attention is directed.

```yaml
eyes:

  focus:
    countdown
```

The single field is `focus`, and in the reference its value is `countdown`.

Eye focus is observable — the camera can see where a character is looking — but it is placed under performance direction because it expresses the character's attention, not their gross bodily movement.

The gaze fixed on the `countdown` is what carries the emotional transition from `controlled` to `disbelief` on screen.

---

# Breathing

The `breathing` group declares the observable breath state.

```yaml
breathing:

  restrained
```

In the reference, breathing is `restrained`.

Breathing is the finest-grained performance signal the platform records. It is observable, but it is emotional in intent: `restrained` breath expresses tension that no gross movement would reveal.

It belongs to performance direction, not blocking, because it communicates internal state through the frame rather than describing where the body travels.

---

# Performance Is Separated From Dialogue

Performance is separated from dialogue.

This is a hard rule, not a stylistic preference.

```text
Dialogue      ──► owned by the Screenplay Engine
Performance   ──► owned by the Shot Planner
```

The Shot Planner directs how a line lands. It never changes the line.

The separation exists for three reasons.

* Dialogue is approved narrative content owned upstream by the Screenplay Engine; the Shot Planner may not rewrite it.
* Emotion must be directable independently of words, because the same line may be delivered `controlled` or in `disbelief`.
* A shot may carry a full emotional transition — `controlled` to `disbelief` — with no dialogue at all.

By separating performance from dialogue, the platform can direct the emotion of a moment without ever reopening what is said in it.

---

# Blocking Versus Performance

The distinction between observable blocking and emotional performance is deliberate and enforced.

| Concern     | Records                | Example fields                          | Example values                        |
| ----------- | ---------------------- | --------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| Blocking    | Observable movement    | `start`, `action`, `end`                | `standing`, `reach_for_override`, `frozen` |
| Performance | Emotional intent       | `emotion`, `eyes`, `breathing`          | `controlled`, `disbelief`, `countdown`, `restrained` |

Blocking is physical and gross.

Performance is emotional and expressed through fine signals — face, gaze, breath.

The two are captured in the same shot but never in the same object.

A well-formed shot answers both questions independently: *what movement occurs* and *what emotion the frame expresses.*

---

# Populating the Shot Schema

Blocking and performance direction populate the shot schema's `blocking` and `emotion` objects defined in Section 4.1.2.

```yaml
shot:
  blocking:
    MAYA:
      start:
        standing
      action:
        reach_for_override
      end:
        frozen
  emotion:
    emotion:
      before:
        controlled
      after:
        disbelief
    eyes:
      focus:
        countdown
    breathing:
      restrained
```

The mapping is direct.

* The blocking `start` / `action` / `end` fields populate the shot schema's `blocking` object.
* The performance direction `emotion`, `eyes`, and `breathing` fields populate the shot schema's `emotion` object.

Both objects are traceable to their originating screenplay scene, and both are immutable once the shot is validated.

No field crosses between the two objects. Observable movement stays in `blocking`. Emotional intent stays in `emotion`.

---

# Invariant P-481

Blocking contains only observable movement.

Any interior, unfilmable, or unobservable state in a `start`, `action`, or `end` field is a violation.

---

# Invariant P-482

Every blocking definition expresses movement as `start`, `action`, and `end`.

---

# Invariant P-483

A shot's blocking declares at most one `action` per character.

---

# Invariant P-484

Performance direction is separated from dialogue and never rewrites it.

---

# Invariant P-485

Performance direction is explicit; emotional state is declared, not inferred downstream.

---

# Invariant P-486

The `emotion` group declares both a `before` state and an `after` state.

---

# Invariant P-487

Blocking populates the shot schema's `blocking` object; performance direction populates the shot schema's `emotion` object; no field crosses between them.

---

# Anti-Patterns

## Anti-Pattern 1 — Interior State in Blocking

Wrong:

```yaml
MAYA:

  start:
    remembering_the_warning

  action:
    reach_for_override

  end:
    frozen
```

Correct:

```yaml
MAYA:

  start:
    standing

  action:
    reach_for_override

  end:
    frozen
```

`remembering_the_warning` is unfilmable. Blocking records only observable movement, so `start` is `standing`.

---

## Anti-Pattern 2 — Emotion Leaking Into Blocking

Wrong:

```yaml
MAYA:

  start:
    standing

  action:
    reach_for_override_in_disbelief

  end:
    frozen
```

Correct:

```yaml
MAYA:

  start:
    standing

  action:
    reach_for_override

  end:
    frozen

emotion:

  before:
    controlled

  after:
    disbelief
```

The action is `reach_for_override`. `disbelief` is an emotional state and belongs in the `emotion` group, not in the blocking `action`.

---

## Anti-Pattern 3 — Performance Rewriting Dialogue

Wrong:

```text
Performance direction changes the spoken line
so it "sounds more shocked."
```

Correct:

```text
Dialogue is unchanged.
Performance direction sets emotion before: controlled, after: disbelief.
```

Emotion is directed through `emotion`, `eyes`, and `breathing`. The line itself is owned by the Screenplay Engine and is never rewritten.

---

## Anti-Pattern 4 — Implied Emotion

Wrong:

```text
Emotion is left blank so the renderer can "decide."
```

Correct:

```yaml
emotion:

  before:
    controlled

  after:
    disbelief
```

Performance is explicitly defined. The engine declares the emotional transition rather than deferring it downstream.

# 4.1.6 — Storyboard & Visual Grammar

## Insynergy Cinematic Thought Leadership Platform

### Master Specification v2.0

---

# Purpose

This section defines the **Storyboard** and the **Visual Grammar** that govern every frame the Shot Planner & Storyboard Engine produces.

The Storyboard defines visual intent.

It answers exactly one question:

> **"What does the audience see, and how is it composed?"**

The Storyboard does not answer *how* the frame is produced.

It never names a lens vendor, a rendering provider, a model, a resolution preset, or a technical parameter.

A storyboard is a description of the picture as a picture — composition, lighting, palette, focal subject, and depth — expressed so that any competent downstream renderer can realize it.

The Visual Grammar defines the fixed structural arc every trailer follows.

It constrains how shots are ordered so that narrative progression maps to a consistent, testable sequence of framings.

Together, the Storyboard and the Visual Grammar convert an approved shot into an explicit, deterministic visual specification.

---

# Design Philosophy

The Storyboard is built on a single separation.

> **Storyboard never specifies technology.**

Visual intent and rendering technology are different concerns, owned by different layers.

Intent is durable. It survives changes of provider, model, and resolution.

Technology is disposable. It is selected downstream, per shot, by the render layer.

The Storyboard therefore records only what is visible and composed, never what machinery produces it.

Three principles follow.

* A storyboard field describes the picture, not the pipeline.
* A storyboard is legible to a human director and to a machine renderer alike.
* A storyboard that names technology is malformed, regardless of its visual quality.

The Visual Grammar is built on a complementary separation.

Every trailer, regardless of subject, follows the same structural arc.

The arc is normative, not advisory. Shot ordering is constrained to it.

---

# Storyboard as Visual Intent

The Storyboard expresses visual intent for a shot.

Visual intent is composed of exactly five concerns.

* composition
* lighting
* palette
* focal_subject
* depth

Each concern describes an observable property of the frame.

None of them describes a technology.

The canonical example of a storyboard frame is the following.

```yaml
frame:

  composition:
    rule_of_thirds

  lighting:
    low_key

  palette:
    graphite

  focal_subject:
    MAYA

  depth:
    deep_focus
```

This frame states its complete visual intent.

It says how the frame is composed, how it is lit, what colors dominate, who the eye rests on, and how depth is handled.

It says nothing about how the image is generated.

> **Storyboard never specifies technology.**

---

# The Frame Fields

Each frame field carries a precise meaning.

The value vocabulary is closed: a frame field takes a named value from a controlled set, not free prose.

## composition

`composition` describes how visual elements are arranged within the frame.

The canonical value is `rule_of_thirds`.

`rule_of_thirds` places the focal subject along the intersections of a three-by-three grid rather than dead center, producing a balanced, deliberately cinematic arrangement.

Composition governs arrangement, never equipment.

---

## lighting

`lighting` describes the intensity and contrast character of illumination.

The canonical value is `low_key`.

`low_key` lighting is dominated by shadow, with a high contrast ratio and controlled highlights, producing a serious, weighted, dramatic mood.

Lighting describes the visible quality of light, never the light source technology.

---

## palette

`palette` describes the dominant color treatment of the frame.

The canonical value is `graphite`.

`graphite` is a restrained, desaturated dark-grey treatment — the disciplined, editorial color signature of the platform.

Palette describes color intent, never a color-grading tool.

---

## focal_subject

`focal_subject` names the element the audience's eye is directed to.

The canonical value is `MAYA`.

`MAYA` is a Character Bible identifier, not an inline description.

The focal subject references continuity by identifier; it never duplicates a character description inside the frame.

---

## depth

`depth` describes how the frame handles focus across distance.

The canonical value is `deep_focus`.

`deep_focus` keeps both foreground and background acceptably sharp, so the whole scene reads as present and legible rather than isolating a single plane.

Depth describes the visible focus field, never a lens or aperture setting.

---

# What a Frame Never Contains

A frame is defined as much by its exclusions as its inclusions.

A storyboard frame shall never contain:

* a rendering provider name;
* a model or engine identifier;
* a resolution, frame rate, or codec;
* a lens focal length or aperture value;
* a prompt string intended for a provider;
* any parameter whose meaning is technological rather than visual.

If a frame names technology, it is malformed and must be rejected before rendering.

> **Storyboard never specifies technology.**

---

# Storyboard Schema

The Storyboard is serialized as a deterministic JSON artifact.

The schema binds the visual intent of the `frame` to the surrounding shot concerns.

```json
{
  "shot_id": "...",
  "frame": {
    "composition": "rule_of_thirds",
    "lighting": "low_key",
    "palette": "graphite",
    "depth": "deep_focus"
  },
  "camera": {},
  "blocking": {},
  "emotion": {}
}
```

The schema has five top-level members.

* `shot_id` binds the storyboard to exactly one shot.
* `frame` carries the visual intent.
* `camera` carries the camera specification.
* `blocking` carries the observable performance.
* `emotion` carries the emotional specification.

The `frame` object carries `composition`, `lighting`, `palette`, and `depth`.

The `focal_subject` is expressed in the visual-intent frame and resolved through continuity; the serialized `frame` object records the four compositional fields shown in the schema.

---

# How the Storyboard Composes Shot Concerns

The Storyboard is a composition point.

It does not own camera, blocking, or emotion.

It references them and binds them to a single frame of visual intent.

```text
shot_id
        │
        ▼
frame  (composition, lighting, palette, depth)
        │
        ▼
camera + blocking + emotion
```

* `camera` is defined by the Camera Language layer.
* `blocking` is defined by the Blocking & Performance layer.
* `emotion` is defined by the observable emotional specification.

The storyboard holds these members together under one `shot_id` so that a single artifact fully describes what the shot looks like.

The empty objects in the schema (`"camera": {}`, `"blocking": {}`, `"emotion": {}`) are composition slots. They are populated by their owning layers and referenced, never redefined, by the storyboard.

---

# Visual Grammar

Every trailer follows the same grammar.

The Visual Grammar is a fixed sequence of narrative beats, each mapped to a required framing.

```text
Beginning

Wide

↓

Middle

Medium

↓

Emotion

Close Up

↓

Decision

Insert

↓

Resolution

Wide
```

The arc is normative.

It defines both the beats and the framing that each beat must use.

---

# The Grammar Arc

The Visual Grammar maps five narrative beats to five framings.

| Beat       | Framing   | Function                                             |
| ---------- | --------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| Beginning  | Wide      | establish the world and the space                    |
| Middle     | Medium    | develop action and relationship                      |
| Emotion    | Close Up  | reveal interior state on the focal subject           |
| Decision   | Insert    | isolate the decisive detail or object                |
| Resolution | Wide      | return to the world and settle the arc               |

The arc opens Wide and closes Wide.

It moves inward from Wide to Medium to Close Up to Insert, then releases back out to Wide.

The inward movement tightens attention toward the emotional and decisive core.

The closing Wide resolves that tension by returning the audience to the world.

---

# Visual Grammar as Normative Structure

The Visual Grammar is a normative structure, not a stylistic suggestion.

> **Every trailer follows the same grammar.**

Consequences follow.

* The five-beat arc is mandatory for every trailer.
* Each beat requires its designated framing.
* The Beginning and Resolution beats are both Wide.
* The framing progression Wide → Medium → Close Up → Insert → Wide is fixed.

A trailer that omits a beat, or assigns a framing that contradicts the grammar, is malformed.

The grammar guarantees that every trailer, regardless of subject, is structurally recognizable and testable against a single template.

---

# How Visual Grammar Constrains Shot Ordering

The Visual Grammar constrains the order in which shots may appear.

Shot ordering must realize the arc.

```text
Wide  →  Medium  →  Close Up  →  Insert  →  Wide
```

* The first framing in the trailer must be Wide.
* Emotional revelation must arrive as a Close Up.
* The decisive detail must arrive as an Insert.
* The final framing must be Wide.

Shots are ordered so that their framings trace this progression.

An ordering that reaches a Close Up before establishing a Wide, or that resolves on anything but a Wide, violates the grammar.

The grammar therefore acts as an ordering invariant: the sequence of framings across a trailer must be a valid realization of the arc.

---

# Storyboard & Grammar Together

The Storyboard and the Visual Grammar operate at two scales.

* The Storyboard governs a single frame's visual intent.
* The Visual Grammar governs the ordering of framings across the whole trailer.

```text

        │
        ▼
Framing per beat
        │
        ▼
Storyboard       (frame-level intent)
```

The grammar assigns each shot its framing role within the arc.

The storyboard then expresses that shot's composition, lighting, palette, focal subject, and depth.

Neither layer specifies technology.

---

# 4.1.7 — Character & Location Continuity

## Insynergy Cinematic Thought Leadership Platform

### Master Specification v2.0

---

# Purpose

This section defines how the Shot Planner & Storyboard Engine preserves **character and location continuity** across every shot in a build.

Continuity is the guarantee that a character looks the same, dresses the same, and inhabits the same physical world from the first shot to the last.

The engine achieves this guarantee through a single mechanism: **reference, never duplication**.

Every shot references the **Character Bible** and the **Location Bible** by immutable identifier.

No shot restates what a character looks like.

No shot restates what a location looks like.

The Shot Planner records *which* character and *which* location a shot uses. It never records *what* they look like.

The visual truth of a character or a location lives in exactly one place, and every shot points to it.

---

# Design Philosophy

The platform adopts the following principle.

> **The planner references identity; it never describes appearance.**

A description written inline is a description that can drift.

If two shots each carry their own copy of a character's appearance, the two copies will eventually disagree. One shot will be edited and the other will not. The character's hair, wardrobe, or face will change between cuts, and the trailer will read as inconsistent.

The engine removes this failure mode structurally.

There is one Character Bible.

There is one Location Bible.

Every shot references them.

Continuity therefore cannot drift, because there is nothing to drift *from* — there is only one source of truth, and every shot resolves against it.

> **The planner never duplicates character descriptions.**

> **Every shot references Location Bible.**

These two statements are not stylistic guidance. They are enforced invariants, validated by the ContinuityValidator (4.1.14) against the shot schema's `continuity` object (4.1.2).

---

# Continuity by Reference

The Shot Planner expresses character continuity as a set of identifiers, not as prose.

A shot does not say "a woman with straight black hair wearing a dark executive suit."

A shot says this.

```yaml
continuity:

  character:
    maya_v1

  wardrobe:
    executive_dark_v1

  hairstyle:
    straight_black_v1
```

Each value is an identifier.

`maya_v1` names the character.

`executive_dark_v1` names the wardrobe.

`straight_black_v1` names the hairstyle.

None of these values describes appearance. Each of them resolves to a description held in the Character Bible.

The planner records the reference. The Character Bible holds the description.

> **The planner never duplicates character descriptions.**

---

# Why References Instead of Descriptions

The engine references identifiers instead of embedding descriptions for four reasons.

## Single Source of Truth

A character's appearance is defined once, in the Character Bible.

Every shot that uses `maya_v1` resolves to the same definition. There is no second copy to fall out of sync.

---

## Drift Elimination

Inline descriptions drift as shots are edited independently.

References cannot drift, because a reference carries no appearance data of its own. It only points.

---

## Deterministic Resolution

Given the same Character Bible, `maya_v1` resolves to the same appearance on every planning run.

Determinism at the continuity layer is a precondition for byte-stable planning artifacts.

---

## Auditability

A reference is testable.

The ContinuityValidator can confirm that `maya_v1` exists in the Character Bible. It cannot meaningfully validate a free-text description embedded in a shot.

References are checkable. Descriptions are not.

---

# The Character Bible

The Character Bible is the single owner of character appearance.

It defines who a character is, what they wear, and how they are groomed.

A shot references the bible through three continuity fields.

```yaml
continuity:

  character:
    maya_v1

  wardrobe:
    executive_dark_v1

  hairstyle:
    straight_black_v1
```

| Field       | Identifier            | Owned By        |
| ----------- | --------------------- | --------------- |
| character   | `maya_v1`             | Character Bible |
| wardrobe    | `executive_dark_v1`   | Character Bible |
| hairstyle   | `straight_black_v1`   | Character Bible |

Every shot references the Character Bible.

The planner never duplicates character descriptions.

---

# The Location Bible

Locations are immutable.

The Location Bible is the single owner of location appearance, and its entries do not change during a build.

An example location is defined as follows.

```yaml
location:

  id:
    operations_center_night

  lighting:
    low_key

  architecture:
    enterprise

  palette:
    graphite
```

The location `operations_center_night` is defined once.

Its lighting is `low_key`.

Its architecture is `enterprise`.

Its palette is `graphite`.

Every shot that takes place in this location references `operations_center_night`. No shot restates its lighting, architecture, or palette.

> **Every shot references Location Bible.**

---

# Location Immutability

Locations are immutable.

Once the Location Bible defines `operations_center_night` with lighting `low_key`, architecture `enterprise`, and palette `graphite`, those values hold for the entire build.

A shot may not override a location's lighting.

A shot may not override a location's architecture.

A shot may not override a location's palette.

The physical world is fixed upstream. The Shot Planner selects which location a shot occupies; it does not redefine the location.

Immutability is what makes a location a reliable anchor for continuity. If every shot could adjust the palette, `graphite` would mean nothing, and the operations center would look different in every cut.

---

# Every Shot References Both Bibles

Continuity is not optional and it is not partial.

Every shot references both the Character Bible and the Location Bible.

```text
Shot
   │
   ├── Character Bible reference
   │        character:  maya_v1
   │        wardrobe:   executive_dark_v1
   │        hairstyle:  straight_black_v1
   │
   └── Location Bible reference
            id:           operations_center_night
            lighting:     low_key
            architecture: enterprise
            palette:      graphite
```

A shot without a Character Bible reference is invalid.

A shot without a Location Bible reference is invalid.

The planner never duplicates character descriptions, and every shot references Location Bible.

---

# Versioned Identifiers

Continuity identifiers are versioned.

The `_v1` suffix is the version marker.

`maya_v1` is version one of the character Maya.

`executive_dark_v1` is version one of the executive-dark wardrobe.

`straight_black_v1` is version one of the straight-black hairstyle.

Versioning exists so that a deliberate change to a character's appearance produces a *new* identifier rather than a silent mutation of an existing one.

```text
Deliberate wardrobe change
        │
        ▼
executive_dark_v1   →   executive_dark_v2   (new identifier)
```

A version is never edited in place.

If Maya's wardrobe must change, the Character Bible gains `executive_dark_v2`. Shots that referenced `executive_dark_v1` are unaffected until they are deliberately re-pointed.

This keeps continuity explicit. Two shots that reference `executive_dark_v1` are guaranteed identical. Two shots that reference different versions are visibly, intentionally different.

---

# Populating the Shot Schema

Continuity references populate the shot schema's `continuity` object, defined in 4.1.2.

The planner writes identifiers into that object; it writes nothing else.

```yaml
continuity:

  character:
    maya_v1

  wardrobe:
    executive_dark_v1

  hairstyle:
    straight_black_v1
```

The `continuity` object is a set of pointers.

It carries the character reference, the wardrobe reference, and the hairstyle reference. It carries no appearance data of its own.

The location reference is resolved through the same object against the Location Bible, so that a shot in `operations_center_night` inherits lighting `low_key`, architecture `enterprise`, and palette `graphite` by reference.

The shot schema is therefore small, stable, and free of duplicated description.

---

# Validation by ContinuityValidator

The `continuity` object is validated by the **ContinuityValidator**, defined in 4.1.14.

The ContinuityValidator confirms that every reference resolves.

```text
Shot continuity object
        │
        ▼
ContinuityValidator
        │
        ├── character reference resolves in Character Bible?
        ├── wardrobe reference resolves in Character Bible?
        ├── hairstyle reference resolves in Character Bible?
        └── location reference resolves in Location Bible?
        │
        ▼
Pass  →  continuity is valid
Fail  →  reject shot
```

If `maya_v1` does not exist in the Character Bible, validation fails.

If `operations_center_night` does not exist in the Location Bible, validation fails.

If a shot embeds an inline character description instead of a reference, validation fails.

The ContinuityValidator does not repair a broken reference. It rejects the shot and returns it for re-planning.

Validation confirms references; it never invents descriptions.

The following invariants are mandatory.

# 4.1.8 — Time Continuity

## Insynergy Cinematic Thought Leadership Platform

### Master Specification v2.0

---

# Purpose

This section defines **time continuity** within the Shot Planner & Storyboard Engine.

Time continuity governs the ordering of temporal state across the shots of a scene.

Its responsibility is to answer exactly one question:

> **"Does time only ever move forward?"**

The engine treats time as a strictly monotonic quantity.

A trailer depicts a sequence of moments. Each moment follows the moment before it.

Time continuity ensures that the visual sequence never contradicts the direction of time.

The engine does not invent temporal state.

It expresses previously approved narrative timing as an ordered, testable, forward-only progression.

---

# Design Philosophy

Time is the one dimension the audience cannot un-see.

A viewer forgives an imperfect lens, a soft shadow, or an unusual color grade.

A viewer will not forgive a clock that runs backward.

The platform therefore elevates time continuity from a stylistic preference to a hard structural invariant.

The governing principle is stated once and never relaxed:

> **"Time may never move backward."**

This principle is absolute.

It applies within a shot.

It applies across the shots of a scene.

It applies across the scenes of the trailer.

Forward-only time is not a heuristic to be balanced against other goals.

It is a boundary condition that every shot plan must satisfy before it can be considered valid.

---

# The Forward-Only Principle

Time continuity is expressed as a single, non-negotiable rule.

> **Time may never move backward.**

Every temporal marker the engine tracks must either advance or hold.

It may never regress.

The engine models time as a monotonically non-increasing countdown or a monotonically non-decreasing clock, depending on the scene's chosen representation.

In both cases the direction of travel is fixed.

Once a moment has passed, the pipeline may never depict an earlier moment as if it were later.

---

# The Countdown Example

The canonical illustration of forward-only time is a countdown timeline.

A countdown is a sequence of decreasing values that terminates at a fixed endpoint.

```yaml
timeline:

  countdown:
    31

↓

27

↓

18

↓

7

↓

STOP
```

The sequence proceeds:

```text
31
 ↓
27
 ↓
18
 ↓
 7
 ↓
STOP
```

Each value is strictly less than the value before it.

```text
31 > 27 > 18 > 7 > STOP
```

The countdown is **monotonically decreasing**.

The gaps between values are irregular — the interval from 31 to 27 is four, from 27 to 18 is nine, from 18 to 7 is eleven — but the direction never changes.

Irregular spacing is permitted.

Reversal is forbidden.

The countdown terminates at `STOP`. Once the timeline reaches `STOP`, no further countdown value may appear.

`STOP` is the temporal floor of the scene.

---

# Monotonicity as the Core Property

A countdown is valid if and only if it is monotonic.

```text
Given a countdown sequence c₀, c₁, c₂, … cₙ:

    c₀ ≥ c₁ ≥ c₂ ≥ … ≥ cₙ

For a strictly decreasing countdown:

    c₀ > c₁ > c₂ > … > cₙ
```

The canonical sequence `31 → 27 → 18 → 7 → STOP` satisfies strict monotonic decrease.

The engine does not require equal intervals.

The engine requires only that no successor value exceed its predecessor.

Monotonicity is the machine-checkable expression of "time may never move backward."

---

# Relationship to Shot Ordering

Time continuity constrains the order in which shots are arranged within a scene.

Each shot carries a temporal marker — a countdown value, a clock reading, or an ordinal moment index.

Shot ordering must be consistent with temporal ordering.

```text
Shot Order         Temporal Marker
─────────────      ────────────────
Shot 1        →    31
Shot 2        →    27
Shot 3        →    18
Shot 4        →    7
Shot 5        →    STOP
```

A shot that appears later in the sequence may never carry a temporal marker earlier than a shot that appears before it.

The engine may reorder shots for cinematic reasons only within the freedom that monotonic time permits.

If two shots share the same temporal marker, they are simultaneous and may be ordered by visual grammar.

If two shots carry different markers, time decides their order absolutely.

---

# Relationship to the Visual Grammar (4.1.6)

Time continuity and the Visual Grammar of section 4.1.6 operate as complementary constraints.

The Visual Grammar governs **how** a moment is composed — framing, lighting, palette, focal subject, depth.

Time continuity governs **when** a moment occurs relative to its neighbors.

The two never conflict, and neither overrides the other.

```text
Visual Grammar (4.1.6)      →   spatial and compositional intent
Time Continuity (4.1.8)     →   temporal ordering intent
```

The Visual Grammar may freely vary composition from shot to shot.

It may not use composition to imply a temporal reversal.

A tighter frame, a darker palette, or a rising camera may express escalating tension as the countdown decreases — but the countdown value itself must continue to fall.

Where the Visual Grammar expresses mounting pressure, time continuity guarantees the pressure resolves in one direction only: forward, toward `STOP`.

---

# Wrong / Correct — Temporal Reversal

## Violation 1 — A Later Shot Shows a Higher Countdown

Wrong:

```text
Shot 3  →  countdown 18
        │
        ▼
Shot 4  →  countdown 27
```

A later shot carries a higher countdown value than an earlier shot.

Time has moved backward. This is forbidden.

Correct:

```text
Shot 3  →  countdown 18
        │
        ▼
Shot 4  →  countdown 7
```

Each successive shot carries a value less than or equal to its predecessor.

---

## Violation 2 — Countdown Restarts After STOP

Wrong:

```text
Shot 5  →  STOP
        │
        ▼
Shot 6  →  countdown 31
```

The timeline restarts after reaching its terminal floor.

`STOP` is final. No value may follow it within the same continuity.

Correct:

```text
Shot 5  →  STOP
        │
        ▼
Scene continuity closes
```

---

## Violation 3 — Non-Monotonic Interleave

Wrong:

```text
31  →  27  →  31  →  18
```

The value returns to 31 after decreasing to 27.

Monotonicity is broken.

Correct:

```text
31  →  27  →  18  →  7
```

The sequence decreases without reversal.

---

## Violation 4 — Composition Implies Reversal

Wrong:

```text
Later shot reuses an earlier moment's exact clock
to imply the scene rewound
```

Correct:

```text
Later shot advances the clock
and expresses tension through the Visual Grammar
```

---

# Flashbacks and Explicit Temporal Modeling

A flashback depicts an earlier moment inside a later position in the sequence.

By default, flashbacks are **forbidden**.

An implicit jump backward in time is indistinguishable from a continuity error and is rejected as one.

A flashback is permitted only when it is **explicitly modeled** as a distinct temporal track.

```yaml
temporal_model:
  primary_timeline:
    direction: forward_only
  flashback:
    declared: true
    track_id:
    enclosing_shot:
    return_marker:
```

An explicitly modeled flashback:

* declares itself as a separate temporal track;
* carries its own monotonic ordering within that track;
* identifies the enclosing shot that frames it;
* defines the return marker at which the primary timeline resumes.

An undeclared backward jump is not a flashback.

It is a violation.

Absent an explicit, declared flashback track, every temporal marker in a scene obeys strict forward-only monotonicity.

---

# The continuity-report.json Artifact

Time continuity is recorded in the `continuity-report.json` artifact.

The report captures the temporal state of every shot and the result of monotonicity checking.

```yaml
continuity_report:
  scene_id:
  temporal_representation: countdown
  markers:
    - shot: 1
      value: 31
    - shot: 2
      value: 27
    - shot: 3
      value: 18
    - shot: 4
      value: 7
    - shot: 5
      value: STOP
  monotonic: true
  direction: forward_only
  terminal_marker: STOP
  flashback_tracks: []
  violations: []
```

The report is a factual record.

If the sequence is monotonic, `monotonic` is `true` and `violations` is empty.

If any successor value exceeds its predecessor, the offending pair is recorded in `violations` and `monotonic` is `false`.

The `continuity-report.json` is traceable to its originating screenplay scene and is immutable once emitted.

---

# Validation by ContinuityValidator

Time continuity is enforced by the `ContinuityValidator`.

The validator consumes the ordered shot markers and applies the monotonicity check.

```text
Ordered Shot Markers
        │
        ▼
ContinuityValidator
        │
        ▼
Monotonicity Check  (cₙ ≤ cₙ₋₁)
        │
        ▼
continuity-report.json
```

The validator's logic is deterministic.

```text
for each adjacent pair (previous, current):
    if current > previous:
        record violation
        mark report not monotonic
if terminal marker reached:
    forbid any subsequent value
```

The validator fails closed.

A scene whose markers are not monotonic — and whose reversals are not accounted for by a declared flashback track — is rejected before any downstream artifact is trusted.

The `ContinuityValidator` never repairs a timeline.

It reports the violation and returns the plan for re-planning from the approved screenplay.

---

# Edge Cases

The engine accounts for the following temporal edge cases.

## Held Time

Two adjacent shots may share the same marker.

```text
27  →  27
```

Equal successive values represent a held moment and are permitted under non-strict monotonicity.

---

## Terminal Floor

`STOP` is the terminal floor.

No value may follow `STOP` within the same continuity.

Reaching `STOP` closes the scene's temporal track.

---

## Irregular Intervals

Intervals between markers need not be uniform.

```text
31 → 27 → 18 → 7 → STOP
```

The gaps `4, 9, 11` are irregular and valid. Only direction is constrained.

---

## Declared Flashback

A backward marker is valid only inside a declared flashback track with a defined return marker.

Any undeclared backward marker is a violation.

---

# Time Continuity Invariants

The following invariants are mandatory.

## Invariant P-541

Time may never move backward.

---

## Invariant P-542

Every temporal marker sequence within a scene is monotonic in its declared direction.

---

## Invariant P-543

A countdown sequence is monotonically non-increasing; the canonical sequence `31 → 27 → 18 → 7 → STOP` is strictly decreasing.

---

## Invariant P-544

A shot appearing later in the sequence never carries a temporal marker that precedes an earlier shot's marker.

---

## Invariant P-545

`STOP` is the terminal floor; no temporal marker may follow it within the same continuity.

---

## Invariant P-546

# 4.1.9 — Render Strategy & Runway Eligibility

## Insynergy Cinematic Thought Leadership Platform

### Master Specification v2.0

---

# Purpose

This section defines how the Shot Planner assigns a **render strategy** to every shot.

A render strategy answers exactly one question:

> **"By what means will this shot be produced?"**

It does not answer *what* the camera captures. That is decided upstream in the shot model.

It does not answer *how* a provider renders pixels. That is decided downstream in the render layer.

The render strategy is the single, explicit bridge between a planned shot and the production mechanism that will later realize it.

Section 4.1.9 establishes six render strategies, the rule that each shot selects exactly one of them, and the eligibility criteria that govern the most expensive strategy: Runway video generation.

The planner assigns strategy.

The planner never renders.

---

# Design Philosophy

The platform treats rendering as a scarce, expensive resource that must be justified per shot.

> **Motion generation is applied only where natural motion carries meaning. Everywhere else, cheaper deterministic strategies are preferred.**

Three principles follow from this stance.

First, **strategy is explicit**. No shot is rendered by an implied or default mechanism. Every shot carries a declared strategy that a reviewer can read and audit.

First-class strategy assignment means the cost profile of an entire trailer is knowable before a single frame is produced.

Second, **strategy is singular**. A shot is produced by one mechanism, never a blend decided at render time. Mixing strategies inside one shot would make cost, timing, and continuity non-deterministic.

Third, **strategy is constrained**. Runway is reserved for shots where generated natural motion is the point. When motion is decorative, structural, or informational, a deterministic strategy is chosen instead.

Cheap by default.

Expensive only when motion matters.

---

# The Six Render Strategies

Each shot selects exactly one strategy from the following closed set.

```yaml
runway_video

animated_still

motion_graphics

title_card

svg_animation

cached_clip
```

This enumeration is exhaustive and closed.

The planner shall not invent a seventh strategy, and it shall not combine two of these into a hybrid mechanism for a single shot.

> **No mixed strategies.**

---

# Strategy — runway_video

`runway_video` produces a shot through AI video generation.

It is the platform's motion-generation strategy and its most expensive one.

It is chosen when the shot depends on **natural, generated motion** — a performance, a physical movement, or a moving camera whose realism carries dramatic weight.

Because it is expensive and rate-governed, `runway_video` is gated by Runway eligibility (below) and bounded by hybrid-rendering ratios (4.1.10).

---

# Strategy — animated_still

`animated_still` produces a shot from a single rendered still image with light, controlled animation applied to it.

Typical treatments include a slow push, a parallax drift, or a subtle atmospheric loop over an otherwise static frame.

It is chosen when the frame must feel alive but does not require generated natural motion.

It delivers cinematic presence at a fraction of the cost of full video generation.

---

# Strategy — motion_graphics

`motion_graphics` produces a shot as designed, animated graphical content.

It covers data-driven and illustrative motion: animated diagrams, charts, kinetic layouts, and other constructed visuals.

It is chosen when the shot's content is **informational or structural** rather than a captured performance.

Motion graphics are authored, not generated as natural motion.

---

# Strategy — title_card

`title_card` produces a shot as a typographic or title composition.

It covers titles, lower thirds, chapter markers, and text-forward frames.

It is chosen when the shot's purpose is to present **language on screen**, not to depict a scene.

A title card is deterministic and cheap to produce.

---

# Strategy — svg_animation

`svg_animation` produces a shot as vector-based animation.

It covers scalable, precise graphical motion: animated logos, iconography, line diagrams, and UI-like vector elements.

It is chosen when the shot benefits from crisp, resolution-independent vector rendering.

SVG animation is fully deterministic and requires no motion generation.

---

# Strategy — cached_clip

`cached_clip` produces a shot by reusing a previously rendered, cached asset.

It is chosen when an equivalent shot has already been produced and its output can be served without re-rendering.

`cached_clip` is the cheapest strategy because it performs no new rendering at all.

It ties directly to the shot cache key and reinforces the determinism boundary: identical planned shots may resolve to a cached result.

---

# Principle — One Strategy Per Shot

Each shot selects exactly one render strategy.

```text
Shot
        │
        ▼
Exactly One Render Strategy
        │
        ▼
Downstream Render Layer
```

A shot is never produced by two strategies at once.

There is no fallback chain evaluated at render time, and there is no per-frame blend of mechanisms inside a single shot.

> **No mixed strategies.**

If a shot appears to require two strategies, that is a signal to decompose it into two shots — each with its own single strategy — not to mix strategies within one shot.

---

# Runway Eligibility

`runway_video` is subject to an eligibility test that the other five strategies are not.

The governing rule is simple.

> **Runway should be used only when natural motion matters.**

When generated natural motion is the reason a shot exists, the shot is eligible for `runway_video`.

When motion is absent, decorative, structural, or informational, the shot is not eligible and must use a deterministic strategy instead.

---

# Eligible for Runway

A shot is eligible for `runway_video` when it depends on natural motion of the following kinds.

```text
facial reaction

reaching movement

walking

camera movement

dramatic reveal
```

* **facial reaction** — a performed emotional response on a character's face.
* **reaching movement** — a physical movement of a character toward or for something.
* **walking** — locomotion whose realism carries the shot.
* **camera movement** — a moving camera whose motion is dramatically meaningful.
* **dramatic reveal** — a motion-driven revelation that lands through movement.

Each of these depends on natural motion that generated video is uniquely suited to produce.

---

# Not Eligible for Runway

A shot is **not** eligible for `runway_video` when its content is one of the following.

```text
diagrams

countdowns

typography

UI

static environment
```

* **diagrams** — informational or structural content; use `motion_graphics` or `svg_animation`.
* **countdowns** — constructed numeric motion; use `motion_graphics`.
* **typography** — language on screen; use `title_card`.
* **UI** — interface depiction; use `svg_animation` or `motion_graphics`.
* **static environment** — a still setting; use `animated_still`.

None of these depend on generated natural motion. Spending Runway on them wastes the platform's most expensive resource.

---

# Decision Table — Shot Characteristic to Strategy

The planner maps observable shot characteristics to a single strategy.

| Shot Characteristic                      | Runway Eligible | Assigned Strategy   |
| ---------------------------------------- | --------------- | ------------------- |
| Facial reaction                          | Yes             | `runway_video`      |
| Reaching movement                        | Yes             | `runway_video`      |
| Walking                                  | Yes             | `runway_video`      |
| Camera movement                          | Yes             | `runway_video`      |
| Dramatic reveal                          | Yes             | `runway_video`      |
| Static environment                       | No              | `animated_still`    |
| Diagrams                                 | No              | `motion_graphics`   |
| Countdowns                               | No              | `motion_graphics`   |
| Typography                               | No              | `title_card`        |
| UI                                       | No              | `svg_animation`     |
| Previously rendered equivalent shot      | No              | `cached_clip`       |

The eligible characteristics resolve to `runway_video`.

The not-eligible characteristics resolve to a deterministic strategy that matches the shot's content.

A shot whose equivalent has already been produced resolves to `cached_clip` regardless of characteristic.

---

# Strategy in the Shot Schema

The assigned strategy is written into the shot as a first-class field.

```yaml

  id:
  scene_ref:
  primary_action:
  camera:
  blocking:
  continuity_refs:

```

The `render_strategy` field is mandatory and single-valued.

It is populated by the Shot Model & Shot Schema defined in 4.1.2, and it carries exactly one enum value from the closed set.

A shot without a `render_strategy` value is invalid and fails closed before rendering.

---

# Constraint by Hybrid Ratios

Strategy assignment is not made shot-by-shot in isolation.

`runway_video` assignment is bounded across the trailer by the hybrid-rendering ratios defined in 4.1.10.


Eligibility is necessary for `runway_video`. It is not sufficient. The ratio ceiling is the final gate.

# Planner Assigns, Never Renders

Render strategy assignment respects the pipeline boundary established in 4.1.1.

```text

        │

        │
        ▼

```

The planner selects the strategy and writes it into the shot.

The planner shall not submit a Runway job, invoke any provider, download or compose an asset, or select provider-specific rendering parameters.

Strategy is a declaration of intent that the downstream render layer later executes.

# 4.1.10 — Hybrid Rendering Rules

## Insynergy Cinematic Thought Leadership Platform

### Master Specification v2.0

---

# Purpose

This section defines **Hybrid Rendering Rules**: the mechanism that caps how much of a trailer may be produced by expensive generated (Runway) footage.

Hybrid rendering is the cost-and-risk discipline of the visual planning control plane.

It exists to answer one question:

> **"How much of this trailer may be generated, and how must the remainder be covered?"**

Section 4.1.9 established *which* shots are Runway-eligible and assigned each shot exactly one render strategy.

This section establishes *how much* generated footage the trailer as a whole may contain, and *what must cover the rest*.

Generated footage is the most expensive, least deterministic, and highest-risk output the pipeline can produce.

Hybrid rendering treats generated footage as a scarce, budgeted resource — never as the default.

The rules here are expressed as **maximum generated durations**, computed as a ratio of total trailer duration, and enforced across the entire trailer rather than shot by shot.

---

# Design Philosophy

The platform treats generated footage as a privilege, not an entitlement.

> **Generated footage is capped by design. What generation does not cover, cheaper deterministic techniques must.**

A trailer is not improved by generating more of it.

A trailer is improved by spending generation where it is justified and covering everything else deterministically.

Three principles follow.

## Generation Is Bounded

The share of a trailer produced by Runway is bounded by an explicit ratio.

The ratio is a **maximum**, never a target and never a minimum.

A trailer that uses less generated footage than the cap is not deficient. It is efficient.

---

## The Remainder Is Always Covered

Duration not covered by generation is not left empty.

It is covered by cheaper, more deterministic techniques.

The remainder is a first-class part of the plan, not a fallback.

---

## The Cap Is Enforced, Not Advised

The ratio is a hard enforcement threshold, not a stylistic suggestion.

A plan that exceeds the cap is rejected before rendering, not corrected afterward.

Enforcement is deterministic and traceable to a metric.

---

# The Hybrid Rendering Ratio

Hybrid rendering caps the **maximum generated duration** of a trailer.

The cap differs between preview and final renders.

```yaml
preview:

  runway_ratio: 0.40

final:

  runway_ratio: 0.50
```

These are **maximum generated durations**, expressed as a fraction of total trailer duration.

In a preview render, at most **40%** of total duration may be Runway-generated footage.

In a final render, at most **50%** of total duration may be Runway-generated footage.

The remaining duration must be covered by cheaper techniques.

---

# Why Preview Is Capped Lower

The preview ratio (`runway_ratio: 0.40`) is deliberately stricter than the final ratio (`runway_ratio: 0.50`).

Preview exists to validate structure, pacing, and continuity — not to spend the full generation budget.

A preview must be cheap enough to iterate on repeatedly.

The final render is permitted a larger generated share because it is produced once, after storyboard approval, when generation spend is justified.

```text
preview  →  runway_ratio 0.40  →  cheap, iterable, structural
final    →  runway_ratio 0.50  →  approved, produced once
```

The cap always rises from preview to final; it never falls.

---

# Coverage of the Remaining Duration

Whatever total duration is not covered by generated footage must be covered by cheaper techniques.

Remaining duration must be covered by:

* still animation
* SVG
* title cards
* overlays

These techniques are deterministic, inexpensive, and fast to produce.

They are not degraded substitutes for generation.

They are the intended, budgeted coverage for the majority of every trailer.

---

# The Coverage Techniques

Each remainder technique carries a distinct role.

## Still Animation

A held image with controlled motion — pan, push, parallax — over a still frame.

Deterministic and inexpensive; conveys motion without generating footage.

---

## SVG

Vector graphics rendered deterministically.

Ideal for diagrams, structural motifs, and precise geometric composition.

---

## Title Cards

Typographic frames carrying text, attribution, or transition beats.

Fully deterministic; zero generation cost.

---

## Overlays

Graphic layers composited over other footage — lower thirds, captions, framing elements.

Deterministic and reusable across shots.

---

# How the Ratio Is Computed

The ratio is computed across the **whole trailer**, not per shot.

`runway_ratio` is the sum of Runway-generated shot durations divided by total trailer duration.

```text
runway_ratio = generated_duration / total_duration

generated_duration = Σ duration(shot) for each Runway shot
total_duration     = Σ duration(shot) for every shot
```

A trailer conforms when:

```text
runway_ratio ≤ preview.runway_ratio   (preview build)
runway_ratio ≤ final.runway_ratio     (final build)
```

The complement of the generated duration is the remainder duration, which the coverage techniques must fill:

```text
remainder_duration = total_duration − generated_duration
```

The remainder is covered by still animation, SVG, title cards, and overlays.

---

# How the Ratio Is Enforced

Enforcement is a whole-trailer gate, not a per-shot check.

```text
Render Strategy assigned per shot   (4.1.9)
        │
        ▼
Sum generated durations
        │
        ▼
Compute runway_ratio
        │
        ▼
Compare against configured cap
        │
        ▼
  ┌─────┴─────┐
  ▼           ▼
Within cap   Exceeds cap
  │           │
  ▼           ▼
Accept plan  Reject before rendering
```

A single shot may be Runway-eligible under 4.1.9 and still be reclassified when the trailer-level cap is exceeded.

Eligibility is a per-shot property; the cap is a trailer-level constraint.

The cap always wins.

---

# Relationship to Render Strategy (4.1.9)

Section 4.1.9 decides render strategy per shot.

This section constrains the *aggregate* of those decisions.

```text
4.1.9  →  which shots MAY be generated (per-shot eligibility)
4.1.10 →  how much MAY be generated in total (trailer-level cap)
```

The two are complementary and ordered.

Render strategy is assigned first; the hybrid ratio is enforced over the result.

When the aggregate exceeds the cap, generated shots must be re-planned to coverage techniques — still animation, SVG, title cards, or overlays — until the trailer conforms.

No mixed strategy within a single shot is ever introduced to satisfy the cap; each shot retains exactly one strategy.

---

# Relationship to Metrics (4.1.13)

The hybrid ratio is observable through the metrics defined in 4.1.13.

Three ratios describe the composition of a trailer.

| Metric           | Meaning                                                      |
| ---------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| `runway_ratio`   | Fraction of total duration produced by generated footage.   |
| `animated_ratio` | Fraction of total duration produced by still animation.     |
| `graphics_ratio` | Fraction of total duration produced by SVG, title cards, and overlays. |

The ratios describe the full trailer:

```text
runway_ratio + animated_ratio + graphics_ratio = 1.0
```

`runway_ratio` is the metric enforced against the hybrid cap.

`animated_ratio` and `graphics_ratio` measure the coverage of the remaining duration.

A conforming trailer always shows `runway_ratio` at or below its configured threshold.

---

# Worked Example

Consider a trailer with a total duration of 60 seconds, built as a final render.

```yaml
total_duration: 60
final:
  runway_ratio: 0.50
```

The maximum generated duration is:

```text
max_generated = 0.50 × 60 = 30 seconds
```

At most 30 seconds may be Runway-generated.

The remaining 30 seconds must be covered by still animation, SVG, title cards, and overlays.

If the same trailer were a preview build, the cap would fall to `0.40`, permitting at most 24 seconds of generated footage and requiring 36 seconds of coverage.

---

---

---

## Invariant P-587

---

# 4.1.11 — Visual Metaphor & Concept Scene Restrictions

## Insynergy Cinematic Thought Leadership Platform

### Master Specification v2.0

---

# Purpose

This section defines how the Shot Planner & Storyboard Engine handles **institutional and abstract concepts**.

Thought leadership content is, by definition, about ideas.

The platform's subject matter is not physical. It concerns decision boundaries, institutional limits, systemic constraints, market forces, and the invisible structures that shape human outcomes.

The engine must render these ideas.

It must never render them as **words on the screen**.

This section establishes exactly one discipline:

> **Abstract concepts must emerge through visual metaphor, never as literal floating text or on-screen labels.**

It also constrains **when** a concept may appear at all, and **how much** of a trailer concept scenes may occupy.

The Shot Planner does not invent the concepts.

It expresses previously approved narrative meaning through disciplined visual language, at the correct moment, in the correct proportion.

---

# Design Philosophy

An idea explained is forgotten.

An idea *felt* is remembered.

The platform rejects the illustrated-lecture model in which an abstract term appears as a caption while a narrator defines it.

That model is informational.

The platform is cinematic.

> **The audience must experience the concept before they are ever told its name.**

A "Decision Boundary" is not a phrase to be printed.

It is an invisible institutional limit that a human being runs into.

The engine's task is to make the audience feel the limit through a character's lived experience first, and only then, if at all, allow the concept to acquire a visual form.

This philosophy produces three hard constraints:

* concepts must be **embodied**, not labeled;
* concepts may appear **only after** the human problem is understood;
* concepts may occupy **only a bounded fraction** of the trailer.

The remainder of this section makes each constraint testable.

---

# Principle — Concepts Emerge Through Metaphor

Institutional concepts must emerge through **visual metaphor**.

They must never appear as floating text, captions, on-screen labels, diagram callouts, or narrated definitions.

The canonical example is preserved exactly.

**Wrong:** Decision Boundary appears as floating text.

**Correct:** Maya encounters an invisible institutional limit that later becomes visually represented as a translucent architectural plane.

The distinction is not stylistic. It is structural.

In the Wrong case, the concept is asserted. The audience is told that a decision boundary exists.

In the Correct case, the concept is **discovered**. The audience watches Maya meet a limit she cannot see, feels the limit through her, and only afterward sees that limit acquire a translucent architectural form.

The metaphor is earned by the human moment that precedes it.

---

# The Metaphor Contract

Every concept scene is governed by a three-part contract.

```text
Human Experience
        │
        ▼
Felt Limit or Force
        │
        ▼
Visual Metaphor
```

The order is mandatory.

* **Human Experience** — a character acts within the world.
* **Felt Limit or Force** — the character encounters the concept as an obstacle, pressure, or boundary, without it being named.
* **Visual Metaphor** — the concept may then acquire a concrete visual representation that grows out of the felt moment.

A concept that begins at the third stage — a visual form with no preceding human experience — violates the contract.

A concept that is named in text at any stage violates the contract.

---

# Prohibited Representations

The following representations of an abstract concept are forbidden.

```text
floating text
on-screen label
caption defining a term
diagram callout
narrated dictionary definition
lower-third terminology
glossary overlay
```

Each of these asserts a concept rather than embodying it.

The engine treats any storyboard frame whose primary subject is printed conceptual terminology as a violation, regardless of typographic quality.

---

# Permitted Representations

The following representations are permitted.

```text
an invisible limit a character runs into
a translucent architectural plane
a physical force acting on a body
a spatial boundary within a location
an environmental pressure
a structural form that emerges from a felt moment
```

Each of these embodies the concept in the physical, observable world the character inhabits.

Permitted representations are always downstream of a human experience. They never open a concept cold.

---

# Concept Scene Timing

A concept scene is a scene whose primary purpose is to express an abstract or institutional idea through metaphor.

Concept scenes are **allowed only after the audience understands the human problem**.

This produces a strict act restriction.

```yaml
concept_scenes:
  allowed: Act 3
  forbidden:
    - Act 1
    - Act 2
```

* **Allowed:** Act 3
* **Forbidden:** Acts 1 and 2

Act 1 establishes the human being.

Act 2 develops the human problem.

Only in Act 3, once the audience understands the human problem, may an abstract concept acquire visual form.

A concept scene placed in Act 1 or Act 2 asks the audience to feel the weight of an idea they have not yet earned. It is prohibited.

---

# Why Concepts Are Deferred

The deferral is not arbitrary.

```text
Act 1 — Who is the human?
        │
        ▼
Act 2 — What is the human problem?
        │
        ▼
Act 3 — What idea does the problem reveal?
```

A concept only carries meaning once the human cost is established.

Maya's collision with an invisible institutional limit only lands because, by Act 3, the audience already understands who Maya is and what she is trying to do.

Introduce the translucent architectural plane in Act 1, and it is set dressing.

Introduce it in Act 3, and it is revelation.

---

# Maximum Concept Ratio

Even in Act 3, concept scenes are bounded.

The platform enforces a maximum ratio of concept scenes to total scenes.

```yaml
concept_scene_ratio: 0.25
```

No more than **0.25** — one quarter — of a trailer's scenes may be concept scenes.

The trailer is about a human being, not about a diagram in motion.

The 0.25 ceiling guarantees that at least three quarters of the trailer remains grounded in human experience.

A trailer that exceeds the ratio has drifted from cinematic thought leadership toward illustrated explanation, and is rejected.

# Constraint on Storyboard Generation

This section constrains storyboard generation defined in **4.1.6**.

When the Storyboard Engine generates frames for a concept scene, it must:

* place the human experience before any metaphorical form;
* express the concept as an observable element of the physical world;
* omit all conceptual terminology from the frame;
* confirm the scene resides in Act 3;
* confirm the running concept ratio remains within the ceiling.

# 4.1.12 — Shot & Storyboard Quality Gates

## Insynergy Cinematic Thought Leadership Platform

### Master Specification v2.0

---

# Purpose

This section defines the **Quality Gates** that stand between visual planning and rendering.

There are exactly two gates.

* The **Shot Quality Gate** validates every individual shot.
* The **Storyboard Quality Gate** validates the storyboard as a whole.

Neither gate generates video.

Neither gate reinterprets the story.

Each gate answers exactly one question.

> **"Is this plan render-ready, or must it be rejected?"**

A gate is not a suggestion.

A gate is a mandatory pass/fail checkpoint.

The Shot Quality Gate confirms that a single shot has stated its purpose, camera, blocking, emotion, continuity, and render strategy, and that it contains exactly one action and at most one camera move.

The Storyboard Quality Gate confirms that the assembled shots read as a coherent visual sequence — composition, continuity, pacing, render balance, concept ratio, and emotional rhythm.

Together these gates are the final control surface of the visual planning control plane defined in Section 4.1.1.

Nothing passes them by inference.

Nothing passes them by degradation.

---

# Design Philosophy

The platform treats rendering as expensive, irreversible, and downstream.

A defect that reaches rendering has already cost money.

A defect that reaches publication has already cost trust.

The Quality Gates exist so that defects are caught at the cheapest possible moment — before a single provider job is submitted.

> **A shot that cannot describe itself cannot be rendered.**

The gates are **fail-closed**.

A missing value is a failure, not a default.

An ambiguous value is a failure, not an interpretation.

An unvalidated shot is a failure, not a candidate.

This philosophy inherits directly from the planning boundary of Section 4.1.1: the engine rejects invalid inputs rather than silently inferring missing meaning. The Quality Gates apply the same discipline to the engine's own outputs.

Every check in both gates traces back to an earlier section that owns the underlying rule.

The gate does not invent new rules.

The gate enforces existing ones at a single, auditable point.

---

# The Two-Gate Model

```text
Shots Planned
        │
        ▼
Shot Quality Gate  ──────► per-shot pass/fail
        │
        ▼
Storyboard Assembled
        │
        ▼
Storyboard Quality Gate ──► whole-sequence pass/fail
        │
        ▼
Render Strategy Execution
```

The Shot Quality Gate runs per shot.

The Storyboard Quality Gate runs once per storyboard, after every shot has individually passed.

A storyboard containing even one failing shot never reaches the Storyboard Quality Gate.

The gates are ordered.

The narrow gate precedes the wide gate.

---

# Failure Blocks Rendering

The governing rule of this section is a single sentence.

> **Failure blocks rendering.**

This is not advisory language.

It is the enforcement of Invariant P-409 from Section 4.1.1: *no shot or storyboard bypasses its Quality Gate before rendering.*

```text
Gate Result = PASS
        │
        ▼
Render Strategy may execute

Gate Result = FAIL
        │
        ▼
Rendering is blocked
        │
        ▼
Return to owning section for correction
```

There is no override path.

There is no "render anyway" flag.

There is no partial-credit rendering of the shots that happened to pass.

A build with a failing gate is a blocked build.

This is the fail-closed boundary of Section 4.1.1 expressed as a runtime gate.

---

# The Shot Quality Gate

Every shot must satisfy **all eight** boolean checks.

Each check is a boolean.

Each boolean must be `true`.

```yaml
purpose_exists: true

camera_defined: true

blocking_defined: true

emotion_defined: true

continuity_valid: true

render_strategy_defined: true

single_action: true

single_camera_move: true
```

Eight `true` values are required.

One `false` value fails the shot.

There is no weighting, no scoring, and no threshold below eight.

The gate is a logical AND across all eight fields.

---

# Shot Gate Checks — Meaning & Ownership

Each boolean is owned by an earlier section. The gate is the enforcement point; the owning section is the source of truth.

## `purpose_exists`

Every shot has an explicit cinematic purpose.

Owned by **4.1.3 — Shot Types & Cinematic Purpose**.

Enforces Invariant P-404: every shot has an explicit cinematic purpose.

A shot with no stated reason to exist fails here.

---

## `camera_defined`

The shot defines lens, angle, movement, and speed.

Owned by **4.1.4 — Camera Language & Constraints**.

A shot whose camera is undefined cannot be captured, and fails here.

---

## `blocking_defined`

The shot describes observable character movement, separated from dialogue.

Owned by **4.1.5 — Blocking & Performance Direction**.

A shot with no blocking direction fails here.

---

## `emotion_defined`

The shot defines its emotional transition, eye focus, and breathing where relevant.

Owned by **4.1.5 — Blocking & Performance Direction**.

A shot with undefined observable emotion fails here.

---

## `continuity_valid`

The shot references a valid Character Bible identifier and a valid Location Bible identifier, and time does not move backward.

Owned by **4.1.7 — Character & Location Continuity** and **4.1.8 — Time Continuity**.

Enforces Invariant P-405 and Invariant P-406.

A shot with a dangling reference or a backward time step fails here.

---

## `render_strategy_defined`

The shot is assigned exactly one render strategy.

Owned by **4.1.9 — Render Strategy & Runway Eligibility**.

Enforces Invariant P-407: every shot is assigned exactly one render strategy.

A shot with zero strategies, or with mixed strategies, fails here.

---

## `single_action`

The shot contains exactly one primary dramatic action.

Owned by **4.1.2 — Shot Model & Shot Schema** and **4.1.3 — Shot Types & Cinematic Purpose**.

Enforces Invariant P-402: every shot contains exactly one primary dramatic action.

A shot carrying two competing actions fails here.

---

## `single_camera_move`

The shot defines at most one camera movement.

Owned by **4.1.4 — Camera Language & Constraints**.

Enforces Invariant P-403: every shot defines exactly one camera movement at most.

A shot chaining multiple camera moves fails here.

---

# Shot Gate Check Traceability

Every check maps to an owning section and a failure consequence.

| Check                     | Owning Section | Failure Consequence                                            |
| ------------------------- | -------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| `purpose_exists`          | 4.1.3          | Shot rejected; no cinematic purpose recorded.                 |
| `camera_defined`          | 4.1.4          | Shot rejected; camera not capturable.                         |
| `blocking_defined`        | 4.1.5          | Shot rejected; no observable movement to direct.              |
| `emotion_defined`         | 4.1.5          | Shot rejected; emotional performance undefined.               |
| `continuity_valid`        | 4.1.7 / 4.1.8  | Shot rejected; bad bible reference or backward time.          |
| `render_strategy_defined` | 4.1.9          | Shot rejected; nothing to hand to the render layer.           |
| `single_action`           | 4.1.2 / 4.1.3  | Shot rejected; ambiguous dramatic intent.                     |
| `single_camera_move`      | 4.1.4          | Shot rejected; uncontrolled camera choreography.              |

Every failure consequence is the same class of outcome: the shot is rejected, and rendering is blocked.

The table records *why*, not *whether*.

# The Storyboard Quality Gate

After every shot individually passes, the storyboard is validated as a whole.

The Storyboard Quality Gate performs **six** checks.

```yaml
composition
continuity
pacing
render balance
concept ratio
emotional rhythm
```

These checks are sequence-level, not shot-level.

A collection of individually valid shots can still fail as a sequence.

The Storyboard Quality Gate exists to catch exactly that failure mode.

---

# Storyboard Gate Checks — Meaning & Ownership

## composition

The storyboard expresses coherent composition, lighting, palette, focal subject, and depth across shots.

Owned by **4.1.6 — Storyboard & Visual Grammar**.

A sequence whose frames do not compose fails here.

---

## continuity

Character, location, and time continuity hold across the whole sequence, not merely within each shot.

Owned by **4.1.7 — Character & Location Continuity** and **4.1.8 — Time Continuity**.

A sequence that is locally continuous but globally inconsistent fails here.

---

## pacing

Shot durations and cut rhythm form a deliberate cadence across the sequence.

Owned by **4.1.6 — Storyboard & Visual Grammar**.

A sequence that stalls or races without intent fails here.

---

## render balance

The mix of render strategies across the sequence respects the hybrid-rendering ratios.

Owned by **4.1.10 — Hybrid Rendering Rules**.

Enforces Invariant P-408: Runway usage remains below the configured hybrid-rendering ratio.

A sequence that over-consumes premium rendering fails here.

---

## concept ratio

The proportion of visual-metaphor and concept shots stays within the configured restriction.

Owned by **4.1.11 — Visual Metaphor & Concept Scene Restrictions**.

A sequence that leans too heavily on abstract concept shots fails here.

---

## emotional rhythm

The emotional progression across shots forms an intentional arc rather than a flat or chaotic line.

Owned by **4.1.6 — Storyboard & Visual Grammar**.

A sequence with no emotional shape fails here.

---

# Storyboard Gate Check Traceability

| Check            | Owning Section | Failure Consequence                                              |
| ---------------- | -------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| composition      | 4.1.6          | Storyboard rejected; frames do not compose as a sequence.       |
| continuity       | 4.1.7 / 4.1.8  | Storyboard rejected; cross-shot continuity broken.              |
| pacing           | 4.1.6          | Storyboard rejected; cadence is unintentional.                  |
| render balance   | 4.1.10         | Storyboard rejected; hybrid-rendering ratio exceeded.           |
| concept ratio    | 4.1.11         | Storyboard rejected; concept-shot restriction exceeded.         |
| emotional rhythm | 4.1.6          | Storyboard rejected; emotional arc absent or incoherent.        |

Every consequence blocks rendering.

The wide gate rejects the sequence; the narrow gate rejects the shot.

Neither renders anything.

---

# Gate Ordering & Fail-Closed Composition

```text
For each shot:
    run Shot Quality Gate
    if any of the eight booleans is false:
        FAIL → block rendering
If all shots pass:
    run Storyboard Quality Gate
    if any of the six checks fails:
        FAIL → block rendering
If both gates pass:
    render strategy execution is permitted
```

The composition is strict.

Shot gate first, storyboard gate second.

Both must pass.

A pass is the only path to rendering.

# The Gate Report

Each gate emits a machine-readable report.

The report is an artifact, not a log line.

## Shot Gate Report

```yaml
shot_gate_report:
  shot_id: "S014-03"
  result: FAIL
  checks:
    purpose_exists: true
    camera_defined: true
    blocking_defined: true
    emotion_defined: false
    continuity_valid: true
    render_strategy_defined: true
    single_action: true
    single_camera_move: true
  failed_checks:
    - emotion_defined
  owning_sections:
    emotion_defined: "4.1.5"
  rendering_blocked: true
```

## Storyboard Gate Report

```yaml
storyboard_gate_report:
  storyboard_id: "SB-2026-07-20-001"
  result: FAIL
  checks:
    composition: true
    continuity: true
    pacing: true
    render balance: false
    concept ratio: true
    emotional rhythm: true
  failed_checks:
    - render balance
  owning_sections:
    render balance: "4.1.10"
  rendering_blocked: true
```

A `PASS` report lists every check as satisfied and sets `rendering_blocked: false`.

A `FAIL` report names the failing checks and the sections that own them, so correction is routed to the right authority.

The report is traceable, immutable once emitted, and attached as planning metadata to the shot or storyboard it describes.

# Wrong / Correct — Shot Gate

## Anti-Pattern 1 — Defaulting a Missing Value

Wrong:

```text
emotion_defined is missing
        │
        ▼
Gate substitutes a neutral default
        │
        ▼
Shot passes
```

Correct:

```text
emotion_defined is missing
        │
        ▼
emotion_defined = false
        │
        ▼
Shot fails → rendering blocked
```

A missing value is a failure, not a default.

---

## Anti-Pattern 2 — Rendering the Shots That Passed

Wrong:

```text
9 of 10 shots pass
        │
        ▼
Render the 9 passing shots now
```

Correct:

```text
9 of 10 shots pass
        │
        ▼
Build is blocked until all 10 pass
```

Failure blocks rendering for the build, not merely for the failing shot.

---

## Anti-Pattern 3 — Two Camera Moves in One Shot

Wrong:

```text
Shot pans, then dollies, then tilts
        │
        ▼
single_camera_move = true (asserted anyway)
```

Correct:

```text
Shot pans, then dollies, then tilts
        │
        ▼
single_camera_move = false
        │
        ▼
Split into separate shots (4.1.4)
```

At most one camera movement per shot. The gate does not negotiate.

## Anti-Pattern 4 — Valid Shots, Invalid Sequence

```text
Every shot passes the Shot Quality Gate
        │
        ▼
Skip the Storyboard Quality Gate
        │
        ▼
Render
```

Correct:

```text
Every shot passes the Shot Quality Gate
        │
        ▼
Run the Storyboard Quality Gate
        │
        ▼
Render only if the sequence passes
```

Individually valid shots can still fail as a sequence.

## Anti-Pattern 5 — Exceeding Render Balance to Finish

Wrong:

```text
render balance would fail
        │
        ▼
```

Correct:

```text
        │
        ▼

        │
        ▼
Re-run the Storyboard Quality Gate
```

The gate enforces the ratio; it does not raise it to accommodate a plan.

# 4.1.13 — Metrics & Caching

## Insynergy Cinematic Thought Leadership Platform

### Master Specification v2.0

---

# Purpose

This section defines how the **Shot Planner & Storyboard Engine** measures a completed shot plan and how it avoids redundant planning work.

It specifies two closely related mechanisms.

* **Metrics** — the quantitative summary the engine emits for every plan it produces.
* **Caching** — the deterministic mechanism by which the engine skips planning when nothing meaningful has changed.

Metrics answer one question:

> **"What did this plan actually produce?"**

Caching answers a different question:

> **"Does this plan need to be produced at all?"**

Both mechanisms are observability and efficiency concerns. Neither may change the meaning of a shot plan. A metric is a description of a plan, never a cause of one. A cache decision is an optimization, never a re-interpretation.

Metrics and caching sit at the boundary between planning and the outside world. They expose the shape of the plan to operators and downstream systems, and they protect the pipeline from doing identical work twice.

---

# Design Philosophy

The platform adopts the following principle.

> **Metrics describe a plan. The cache decides whether a plan must be recomputed. Neither may alter the plan.**

Three commitments follow from this principle.

**Metrics are derived, never authored.**
Every metric is a pure function of the emitted `shot-list.json` and its render-strategy assignments. No metric is entered by hand, inferred, or estimated. Recomputing the metrics from the same plan must yield identical values.

**Caching is deterministic.**
The decision to skip planning is made from a single content-addressed key. The same inputs always produce the same key, and the same key always represents the same plan. There is no fuzzy matching and no partial reuse.

**The cache is an optimization, not an authority.**
A cache hit reproduces exactly the plan that a full run would have produced. A cache miss recomputes from approved inputs. In either case the resulting plan is byte-identical to a plan produced with no cache at all. The cache never becomes a source of truth in its own right.

These commitments extend the determinism boundary established in 4.1.1: repeated planning with identical inputs shall produce an identical shot plan. Caching is the mechanism that lets the engine honor that boundary without paying the cost of recomputation.

---

# Metrics

At the completion of a plan, the engine emits a metrics object summarizing the shot plan.

```json
{
  "shots": 28,
  "average_duration": 2.7,
  "runway_ratio": 0.43,
  "animated_ratio": 0.31,
  "graphics_ratio": 0.26
}
```

This object is the canonical, machine-readable summary of a completed plan. It is emitted alongside the planning artifacts and is traceable to the `shot-list.json` from which it was derived.

Every field is a derived quantity. The engine computes each field from the emitted shot list and its render-strategy assignments. No field is authored, estimated, or supplied by an operator.

---

# The Metrics Object, Field by Field

The metrics object contains exactly five fields.

| Field              | Value | Meaning                                                        |
| ------------------ | ----- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| `shots`            | 28    | Total number of shots decomposed from the approved screenplay. |
| `average_duration` | 2.7   | Mean shot duration, in seconds, across all shots.             |
| `runway_ratio`     | 0.43  | Fraction of shots assigned the Runway render strategy.        |
| `animated_ratio`   | 0.31  | Fraction of shots assigned the animated render strategy.      |
| `graphics_ratio`   | 0.26  | Fraction of shots assigned the graphics render strategy.      |

Each field is described in detail below.

---

## `shots` — 28

The total count of shots in the plan.

```text
shots = count(shot-list.json entries)
```

This value equals the number of entries in `shot-list.json`. Every shot maps to exactly one screenplay scene, and one scene may decompose into many shots, so `shots` is always greater than or equal to the number of screenplay scenes.

In the reference plan, the engine decomposed the approved screenplay into **28** shots.

`shots` is the denominator for every ratio field. A ratio is only meaningful relative to the shot count that produced it.

---

## `average_duration` — 2.7

The mean shot duration, expressed in seconds.

```text
average_duration = sum(shot.duration) / shots
```

This value summarizes pacing. A low average duration indicates rapid, tightly cut pacing; a higher average indicates longer held shots.

In the reference plan the mean shot duration is **2.7** seconds. Across 28 shots this implies a plan on the order of roughly seventy-five seconds of screen time, consistent with a short cinematic trailer.

`average_duration` describes the plan. It does not constrain it. Duration limits, where they exist, are enforced by the shot model and the quality gates, not by this metric.

---

## `runway_ratio` — 0.43

The fraction of shots assigned the **Runway** render strategy.

```text
runway_ratio = count(shots where strategy == runway) / shots
```

Runway is the most expensive render strategy. `runway_ratio` is therefore the single most operationally significant metric in the object: it is a direct proxy for rendering cost and for exposure to the external Runway provider.

In the reference plan, **0.43** of shots — 43 percent — are assigned the Runway strategy.

`runway_ratio` is not merely descriptive. It is the metric that the hybrid-rendering caps of 4.1.10 govern. Its relationship to those caps is defined below.

---

## `animated_ratio` — 0.31

The fraction of shots assigned the **animated** render strategy.

```text
animated_ratio = count(shots where strategy == animated) / shots
```

Animated shots are produced by non-Runway animation strategies. They are cheaper than Runway shots and are used where motion is required but photoreal generation is not justified.

In the reference plan, **0.31** of shots — 31 percent — are animated.

---

## `graphics_ratio` — 0.26

The fraction of shots assigned the **graphics** render strategy.

```text
graphics_ratio = count(shots where strategy == graphics) / shots
```

Graphics shots are the cheapest strategy. They cover titles, motion graphics, and concept visuals that do not require generative rendering.

In the reference plan, **0.26** of shots — 26 percent — are graphics.

---

# Ratio Composition

The three render-strategy ratios partition the shot plan.

```text
runway_ratio   + animated_ratio + graphics_ratio
0.43           + 0.31           + 0.26            = 1.00
```

Every shot is assigned exactly one render strategy, and no mixed strategies are permitted, so the three ratios describe a complete and non-overlapping partition of the plan. They sum to one.

This is a direct consequence of the render-strategy boundary in 4.1.1: each shot selects exactly one render strategy. The metrics object simply reports the resulting distribution.

```text
28 shots
        │
        ├── 43% Runway   → most expensive
        ├── 31% Animated → moderate cost
        └── 26% Graphics → least expensive
```

The ordering of cost — Runway, then animated, then graphics — is the reason `runway_ratio` is singled out for enforcement.

---

# runway_ratio and the Hybrid Caps

The `runway_ratio` metric does not stand alone.

It is the observable quantity that the **hybrid-rendering rules of 4.1.10** constrain.

> **runway_ratio must remain below the configured hybrid-rendering threshold.**

Section 4.1.10 defines hybrid-rendering caps that limit how large a share of a plan may use the Runway strategy. The purpose of those caps is to bound rendering cost and provider exposure. `runway_ratio` is precisely the number those caps measure.

The relationship is one of measurement and enforcement.

* **4.1.10** sets the ceiling.
* **4.1.13** reports the measured value.
* The observed `runway_ratio` **must stay under** the configured threshold.

```text
runway_ratio (measured, 4.1.13)   <   hybrid cap (configured, 4.1.10)
0.43                              <   threshold
```

In the reference plan, a `runway_ratio` of **0.43** is a value the engine reports; it is compliant only if it remains below the configured hybrid cap. If a plan's `runway_ratio` were to reach or exceed the cap, the plan violates the hybrid-rendering rule regardless of any other quality it may possess.

This preserves the invariant from 4.1.1 that Runway usage remains below the configured hybrid-rendering ratio. The metric is how that invariant is observed; the cap is how it is enforced. The metric never changes the cap, and the cap is never inferred from the metric.

---

---

## Invariant P-642

---

## Invariant P-643

## Invariant P-644

# Caching

The engine caches completed shot plans to avoid redundant planning.

The cache is keyed on a single deterministic identifier.

```text
sha256(

screenplay_hash

+

shot_planner_version

+

camera_profile

+

character_bible

+

location_bible
)
```

This key is the content address of a shot plan. It is computed by hashing the concatenation of exactly five components with SHA-256.

If the inputs are unchanged, the key is unchanged, and:

> **Shot planning is skipped.**

The cache is the operational realization of the determinism boundary. If identical inputs always produce an identical plan, then a plan produced from inputs the engine has already seen need not be recomputed — it can be reproduced from cache.

---

# The Cache Key, Component by Component

The cache key is composed of exactly five components. Every component is part of the key, and no other input contributes to it.

| Component              | Captures                                                       |
| ---------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- |
| `screenplay_hash`      | The approved screenplay content the plan decomposes.           |
| `shot_planner_version` | The version of the planning logic that produced the plan.      |
| `camera_profile`       | The camera language and constraints applied to the plan.       |
| `character_bible`      | The character continuity references the plan depends on.       |
| `location_bible`       | The location continuity references the plan depends on.        |

Each component is load-bearing. A change to any one of them changes the key and therefore forces a re-plan.

**`screenplay_hash`** — the content of the approved screenplay. If the story or scene content changes, the plan must change.

**`shot_planner_version`** — the version of the planner itself. If the planning logic changes, an identical screenplay may decompose differently, so the version participates in the key. This is what prevents a stale plan from a previous engine version from being served after an upgrade.

**`camera_profile`** — the camera language and constraints. A different camera profile produces different camera plans, so the profile is part of the key.

**`character_bible`** — the character continuity source. Because the planner references character identifiers, a change to the bible can change what those references resolve to.

**`location_bible`** — the location continuity source. As with the character bible, a change here can change resolved continuity.

Together these five components are the complete set of inputs that determine a shot plan. Hashing them yields a stable, deterministic content address.

---

# Cache Hit and Miss Behavior

The cache decision is binary and content-addressed.

```text
Compute cache key
        │
        ▼
Key present in cache?
   │            │
  yes          no
   │            │
   ▼            ▼
Cache HIT     Cache MISS
   │            │
   ▼            ▼
Skip planning  Run full planning
Reuse plan     Emit new plan
               Store under key
```

**Cache hit.**
The computed key already exists in the cache. Every one of the five input components is unchanged. The engine reproduces the previously emitted plan and **shot planning is skipped**. The reused plan is byte-identical to what a full run would have produced, because the inputs that produced it are identical.

**Cache miss.**
The computed key is absent from the cache. At least one of the five input components has changed, or this input combination has never been planned. The engine runs full planning from the approved inputs, emits a new plan, and stores it under the newly computed key.

Because the key is deterministic, a hit is exact. There is no partial reuse and no approximate match. The engine either has the plan for these exact inputs or it does not.

---

# Incremental Planning

The cache implements an incremental principle.

> **If unchanged, shot planning is skipped.**

This is the efficiency counterpart to the determinism boundary of 4.1.1. Determinism guarantees that identical inputs *would* produce an identical plan; caching lets the engine *avoid producing it again*.

The two statements are equivalent under the cache key.

* **Determinism (4.1.1):** identical inputs ⇒ identical plan.
* **Caching (4.1.13):** identical inputs ⇒ identical key ⇒ skip planning.

Because the key is built from exactly the inputs the determinism boundary names — screenplay, planner version, camera profile, character bible, location bible — a cache hit is provably safe. The skipped plan cannot differ from the plan a full run would have produced.

Skipping planning is therefore never a shortcut that trades away correctness. It is the direct application of determinism to avoid wasted work.

# Cache Invalidation — Anti-Patterns

Cache invalidation must follow from the key, never from ad-hoc judgment.

## Anti-Pattern 1 — Invalidation by Time

Wrong:

```text
Plan is 24 hours old
        │
        ▼
Force a re-plan on a timer
```

Correct:

```text
Compute cache key from the five inputs
        │
        ▼
Key unchanged → serve cached plan
Key changed   → re-plan
```

Age is not an input to the plan. A plan does not become incorrect because time has passed; it becomes stale only when one of its inputs changes.

---

## Anti-Pattern 2 — Partial Invalidation

Wrong:

```text
Character bible changed
        │
        ▼
Re-plan only the shots that "look affected"
```

Correct:

```text
Character bible changed
        │
        ▼
Cache key changes
        │
        ▼
Re-plan the whole shot plan
```

The cache key is computed over the whole input set and addresses the whole plan. A change to any component changes the key and forces a full re-plan. There is no partial reuse.

---

## Anti-Pattern 3 — Ignoring the Planner Version

Wrong:

```text
Planner upgraded to a new version
        │
        ▼
Serve the plan cached under the old version
```

Correct:

```text
Planner upgraded to a new version
        │
        ▼
shot_planner_version changes
        │
        ▼
Cache key changes → re-plan
```

`shot_planner_version` is part of the key precisely so that a logic change forces a re-plan. Serving a plan produced by a previous planner version silently violates determinism.

---

## Anti-Pattern 4 — Editing a Cached Plan in Place

Wrong:

```text
Inputs changed
        │
        ▼
Mutate the cached plan to match
```

Correct:

```text
Inputs changed
        │
        ▼
Cache key changes
        │
        ▼
Emit a new plan under the new key
```

A cached plan is immutable, consistent with the artifact immutability boundary of 4.1.1. A material change in inputs requires a new planning generation under a new key, not an in-place edit of a plan addressed by the old key.
